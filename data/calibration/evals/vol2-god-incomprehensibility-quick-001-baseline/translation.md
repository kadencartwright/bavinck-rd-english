§ 20. THE INCOMPREHENSIBILITY OF GOD.

1. Mystery is the vital element of dogmatics. To be sure, Scripture understands by μυστηριον no abstract supernatural truth in the Roman sense; but it is equally far removed from the thought that the believer would understand and comprehend the revealed mysteries in a scientific sense, cf. vol. I, 532. Rather, the knowledge which God has revealed of Himself in nature and Scripture far surpasses the representation and concept of man. Insofar as it does, everything with which dogmatics deals is mystery. It has, after all, nothing to do with finite creatures, but from beginning to end elevates itself above all creature to the Eternal and the Infinite Himself. Immediately as soon as it begins, it faces the Incomprehensible. From Him it takes its beginning, for from Him are all things. But also in the further loci, when it descends to the creatures, it considers these only in their relation to God, as they are from and through and to Him. Thus the knowledge of God is the only dogma, the exclusive content of the whole dogmatics. All doctrines that are treated in it, concerning the world, concerning man, concerning Christ, etc., are but the explication of the one, central dogma of the cognitio Dei. From Him everything is viewed. Under Him everything is subsumed. To Him everything is traced back. It is always God and God alone whose glory in creation and recreation, in nature and grace, in world and church it has to contemplate and describe. It is His knowledge alone which it must display and set forth. Thereby dogmatics does not become an arid, academic science, without fruit for life. The more it contemplates Him whose knowledge is its only content, the more it will pass over into admiration and worship. If only it never forgets to think and speak about _things_ instead of about _words_; if only it remains a Theology der Thatsachen and does not degenerate into a Theology der Rhetorik, it is, as a scientific description of the knowledge of God, also highly fruitful for life. For the knowledge of God in Christ is life itself, Ps. 89:16; Isa. 11:9; Jer. 31:34; John 17:3. Therefore Augustine desired to know nothing else and nothing more than God and himself: Deum et animam scire cupio. Nihilne plus? Nihil omnino. Therefore Calvin began his Institutes with the knowledge of God and of ourselves. And therefore the Catechism of Geneva gave to the first question, quis humanae vitae praecipuus est finis? as answer: ut Deum, a quo conditi sunt homines, ipsi noverint, cf. also the Catech. of Westminster.
