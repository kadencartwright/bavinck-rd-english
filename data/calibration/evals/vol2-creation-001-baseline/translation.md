§ 29. CREATION.


1. The execution of God’s counsel begins with creation. This is the beginning and foundation of all revelation of God and therefore also the foundation of all religious and ethical life. The Old Testament creation narrative is so sublimely beautiful that it finds its equal nowhere and has been praised without controversy by all, even by naturalists such as Cuvier and von Humboldt. Das erste Blatt der mosaischen Urkunde hat mehr Gewicht als alle Folianten der Naturforscher und Philosophen (Jean Paul). And thereafter creation repeatedly comes to the foreground in the history of revelation. True religion distinguishes itself from the first moment from all other religions in that it conceives the relation of God to world and man as that of the Creator to His creature. The thought of a being outside of and independent from God occurs nowhere in Scripture. God is the sole and absolute cause of all that is. He has created all things by His Word and Spirit, Gen. 1:2, 3, Ps. 33:6, 104:29, 148:5, Job 27:3, 33:4, Isa. 40:13, 48:13, Zech. 12:1, John 1:3, Col. 1:16, Heb. 1:2, etc. Nothing stood over against Him; no matter that binds Him, no power that limits Him. He speaks and it is, Gen. 1:3, Ps. 33:9, Rom. 4:17. He is the unrestricted possessor of heaven and earth, Gen. 14:19, 22, Ps. 24:2, 89:12, 95:4, 5. There is no limit to His power; He does whatever pleases Him, Isa. 14:24, 27, 46:10, 55:10, Ps. 115:3, 135:6. All things are from and through and to Him, Rom. 11:36, 1 Cor. 8:6, Heb. 11:3. The world is the product of His will, Ps. 33:6, Rev. 4:11; it is the revelation of His virtues, Prov. 8:22ff., Job 28:23ff., Ps. 104:1, 136:5ff., Jer. 16:12 and has its end in His glory, Isa. 43:17, Prov. 16:4, Rom. 11:36, 1 Cor. 8:6. This doctrine of creation, which occupies such an all-controlling place in Scripture, is not presented as a philosophical explanation of the world-problem. It certainly also gives an answer to the question concerning the origin of all things; but it has above all a religious-ethical significance. No right relation to God is conceivable except on its foundation. It places us in that relation to God in which we ought to stand. And therefore it is of eminent practical value. It serves to bring out the greatness, the almighty power, the majesty and at the same time the goodness, the wisdom, the love of God, Ps. 19, Job 37, Isa. 40; and therefore it strengthens faith, confirms trust in God, comforts in suffering, Ps. 33:6f., 65:6ff., 89:12, 121:2, 134:5, Isa. 37:16, 40:28ff., 42:5, etc.; it stirs up to praise and thanksgiving, Ps. 136:3ff., 148:5, Rev. 14:7; disposes to humility and meekness and makes man feel his insignificance and nothingness over against God, Job 38:4ff., Isa. 29:16, 45:9, Jer. 18:6, Rom. 9:20.


2. The doctrine of creation is known only from revelation; it is understood by faith, Heb. 11:3. The Romanists indeed teach that it can also be traced from nature by reason, Thomas, Sent. II dist. 1 qu. 1 art. 2. S. c. Gent. II 15. 16. Kleutgen, Philos. der Vorz. II² 795 f. Scheeben, Dogm. II 5. 6, and the Vaticanum even elevated this to a dogma, de fide c. 2 can. 2. But the history of religions and of philosophy does not argue for this. Mohammedanism indeed teaches creation from nothing but has borrowed this from Judaism and Christianity, Zöckler, Gesch. der Beziehungen zw. Theol. und Naturw. 1877 I 426 f. The heathen cosmogonies are all polytheistic and at the same time theogony; they all assume an Urstof, whether this is conceived as a chaos, as a personal principle, as an egg or the like; and finally they are either more emanationist, so that the world is an effluence of God, or more evolutionist, so that the world increasingly deifies itself, or more dualistic, so that the world is a product of two hostile principles, Wuttke, Die Kosmogonien der heidn. Völker vor der Zeit Jesu und der Apostel, Haag 1850. Lenormant, Les origines de l’histoire d’après la Bible et les traditions des peuples orientaux, Paris 1880. Zöckler in Herzog² 13, 631 f. Also the Chaldean Genesis, which otherwise offers remarkable parallels with that of the O.T., makes no exception to this. It is at the same time theogony and lets Bel form the world from the tiamat, which chaotically conceals everything in itself, Saussaye, Lehrb. der Rel. I 341 f. Greek philosophy seeks either materialistically the origin of things in a material element (Ionic School, Atomists), or pantheistically in the one, eternal, immutable being (Elea) or in the eternal becoming (Heraclitus, Stoa). Even Anaxagoras, Plato and Aristotle did not rise above a dualism of spirit and matter; God is no creator, but at most a former of the world (δημιουργος). The scholastics indeed asserted that Plato and Aristotle taught a creation from nothing, but this was rightly contested by others, among others by Bonaventura, Sent. II dist. 1 p. 1 art. 1 qu. 1. The Greeks knew a φυσις (natura) and κοσμος (mundus) but no κτισις (creatura). Christianity achieved victory over this heathen theogony and cosmogony in the struggle against Gnosticism, which, in order to explain sin, assumed next to the highest God a lower God or an eternal ὑλη. But yet the paganistic explanations of the origin of things repeatedly surfaced again in the Christian centuries. Already in the book of Wisdom 11:17 it is said that God’s almighty hand created the world from ἀμορφος ὑλη; and the same expression occurs also in Justin Martyr, Apol. I 10. 59. But Justin need thereby have in view the later so-called creatio secunda and teaches elsewhere expressly also the creation of matter, Dial. c. Tr. 5. Coh. ad. Gr. 23, cf. Semisch, Justin der M. II 336. As in the second century Gnosticism, so after Nicea especially Manichaeism arose, which likewise, in order to explain sin, assumed next to the good God an originally evil being, Augustine in many writings, c. epist. Manich., c. Faustum, c. Fortunatum, etc. Flügel, Mani, seine Lehre und seine Schriften, Leipzig 1862. Kessler in Herzog² 9, 233 f. This dualism found wide dissemination in Christendom, even as far as the Priscillianists in Spain, and upon renewal in the Middle Ages appeared in many sects, such as the Bogomils and Cathars. But besides dualism, pantheism also found its interpreters.
