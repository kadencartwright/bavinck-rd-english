§ 26. THE ESSENTIAL NAMES OF GOD.


A. God as Being.


1. In some, the locus on the Trinity precedes that on the virtues of God. Damascenus, de fide orthod. I 6 sq., Lombardus, Sent. I dist. 2 sq., Bonaventura, Brevil. I c. 2 sq., Martyr, Loci Comm. p. 36 sq. enz.; and Frank even raises serious objection to the reverse order, Syst. der chr. Wahrh. I 151 f. If the treatment of the virtues of God before the doctrine of the Trinity were intended to climb gradually from natural theology to revealed theology and from the natural to the Christian concept of God, it would undoubtedly deserve disapproval. But this is by no means the case. In the doctrine of the perfections of God, the Divine nature is treated as it has been revealed to us in Scripture, is confessed by the Christian faith, and will presently appear in the locus on the Trinity to exist in a threefold manner. In order to understand in that locus that Father, Son, and Spirit participate in the same Divine nature, it is necessary to know what that Divine nature contains in itself and in what it is distinguished from all created nature. Scripture also leads us in this. The essence of God is taught to us there earlier and more clearly than His Trinitarian existence; the Trinity comes to clear revelation only in the N. T.; the names Ihvh, Elohim precede those of Father, Son, and Spirit. The first thing that Scripture causes us to know of God is that He has a proper being and life, distinguished from all creatures, subsistent and independent. He has a proper essence, a proper φυσις, natura, substantia, essentia, not in distinction from His virtues, but approaching us and becoming known in all virtues and perfections. He bears proper names, which belong to no creature. Among these the name of Ihvh stands at the top, Ex. 3:14. By this name He is designated as the One who is and will be what He was, who eternally remains like Himself in relation to His people. He exists from Himself. He existed before all things, and these exist only through Him, Ps. 90:2, 1 Cor. 8:6, Rev. 4:11. He is ‎‏אָדוֹן‏‎ κυριος, δεσποτης in the absolute sense, Lord of the whole earth, Ex. 23:17, Deut. 10:17, Josh. 3:13. He is from nothing, everything is dependent on Him, Rom. 11:36. He kills and makes alive, creates light and darkness, peace and evil, Deut. 32:39, Isa. 45:5-7, 54:16. He does with the host of heaven and the inhabitants of the earth according to His will, Dan. 4:35, so that men are in His hand as clay in the potter's hand, Isa. 64:8, Jer. 18:1 ff., Rom. 9:21. His counsel, His good pleasure is the ultimate ground of all that is and happens, Ps. 33:11, Prov. 19:21, Isa. 46:10, Matt. 11:26, Acts 2:23, 4:28, Eph. 1:5, 9, 11. He therefore does all things for His own sake, for His name's sake, for the sake of His glory, Deut. 32:27, Josh. 7:9, 1 Sam. 12:22, Ps. 25:11, 31:4, 79:9, 106:8, 109:21, 143:11, Prov. 16:4, Isa. 48:9, Jer. 14:7, 21, Ezek. 20:9, 14, 22, 44. He also has need of nothing, is all-sufficient, Job 22:2, 3, Ps. 50:19 ff., Acts 17:25, and has life in Himself, John 5:26. And so He is the first and the last, the alpha and the omega, who is and who was and who is to come, Isa. 41:4, 44:6, 48:12, Rev. 1:8 enz.; absolutely independent, not only in His existence, but in consequence thereof also in all His virtues and perfections, in all His decrees and acts. Independent is He in His understanding, Rom. 11:34, 35, in His will, Dan. 4:35, Rom. 9:19, Eph. 1:5, Rev. 4:11, in His counsel, Ps. 33:11, Isa. 46:10, in His love, Hos. 14:5, in His power, Ps. 115:3 enz. And thus all-sufficient in Himself, and receiving nothing from outside Himself, He is on the contrary the only source of all being and life, of all light and love, the abundant fountain of all goods, Ps. 36:10, Acts 17:25. This independence of God is recognized more or less by all men. The heathen certainly drag the divine down into the creaturely and teach a theogony, but nevertheless often assume again behind and above their gods a power to which everything is subjected in the absolute sense. Many speak of nature, chance, fate, or fortune as a power elevated above all things; and philosophers designate God preferably with the name of the Absolute. In Christian theology this virtue of God bore the name of αὐυταρκεια, aseitas, omnisufficientia, independentia, magnitudo. In the East one spoke of θεος ἀναρχος, ἀναιτιος, ἀγεννητος and called God preferably αὐτογεννητος, αὐτοφυης, αὐτουσιος, αὐτοθεος, αὐτοφως, αὐτοσοφια, αὐτοαρετη, αὐταγαθος enz., cf. Suicerus, Thes. Eccl. s. v. God is all that He is through Himself. He is ipsa per se bonitas, sanctitas, sapientia, vita, lux, veritas etc. As was said earlier, the church fathers, following Philo, mostly proceeded from the name Ihvh in the description of God. That was His essential name par excellence. God was the Being. In this name: I shall be who I shall be, everything is enclosed. All other virtues of God were derived from it. God is summum esse, summum bonum, summum verum, summum pulchrum. He is the perfect, the highest good being, quo esse aut cogitari melius nihil possit. He has the whole being in Himself, He is an infinite and boundless sea of being. Si bonum, si magnum, si beatum, si sapientem vel quidquam tale de Deo dixeris, in hoc verbo instauratur quod est Est. Nempe hoc est ei esse, quod haec omnia esse. Si et centum talia addas, non recessisti ab esse; si ea dixeris, nihil addixisti; si non dixeris nihil minuisti, Bernardus, de consid. I c. 6. Scholasticism joined itself to this, Anselmus, Monol. 6. Lombardus, Sent. I dist. 8. Thomas, S. Theol. I qu. 2 art. 3 en qu. 3 en qu. 13 art. 11. S. c. Gent. I c. 16 sq. and treated this virtue also under the name of the infinitas or spiritualis magnitudo Dei, Thomas, S. Theol. I qu. 7. S. c. Gent. I c. 43, or under that of the aseitas Dei, by which was indicated that God as summa substantia per se ipsam aut ex se ipsa est quidquid est, Anselmus, Monol. 6. Later Roman theologians likewise mostly proceed from this aseitas or independentia, Petavius, de Deo I c. 6. Theol. Wirceb. III p. 38 sq. Perrone, Prael. theol. II p. 88-90. Jansen, Prael. theol. dogm. II 26 sq. The Reformation brought no change in this. Luther also describes God on the ground of the name Ihvh as the absolute Being; He is pure being. But yet Luther does not busy himself long with abstract, metaphysical descriptions and proceeds quickly from the Deus absconditus to the Deus revelatus in Christus, Köstlin, Luthers Theol. II 302 f. Melanchthon in his Loci describes God as essentia spiritualis. The Lutherans have mostly adopted this description but then often added the further determination infinita, a se subsistens or independens to it, Schmid, Dogm. der ev. Luth. K. § 17. Among the Reformed this perfection of God comes even more to the foreground, although the name aseitas is later mostly exchanged for that of independentia. Aseitas only expresses the independence of God in His existence, but independentia has a broader sense and includes that God is independent in everything, in His being, in His virtues, in His decrees, in His works. While the earlier ones therefore mostly proceeded from the name Ihvh, Hyperius, Meth. Theol. p. 87, 135. Sohnius, Op. II 48. III 261. Polanus, Synt. Theol. p. 135 enz., later independentia mostly appears as the first of the incommunicable properties, Mastricht, Theol. II c. 3, Heidegger, Corpus Theol. III § 30. Maresius, Syst. theol. loc. 2 § 17. Marck, Merg der Godg. IV § 20. L. Meyer, Verhand. over de Godd. eigensch. 1783 I bl. 39-110 enz. When now in Scripture God ascribes to Himself this aseitas, He thereby makes Himself known as the absolute being, as the Being in the absolute sense. By this perfection He is immediately essentially and absolutely distinguished from all creature. After all, every creature is not a se but ab alio, and is and has thus nothing of itself; it is absolutely dependent in its origin and thus also in all its further existence and development. But God is, according to this name, only from Himself, a se, not in the sense as if He had caused Himself, but as from eternity to eternity being who He is, being not becoming, the absolute being, the fullness of being, and thus also eternal and absolutely independent in His existence, in His virtues, in all His works, the first and the last, the only cause and final end of all things. In this aseity of God all other virtues are enclosed; they are given with it and from it the rich, manifold unfolding. And yet, while by this virtue the immeasurable distinction between Creator and creature immediately comes to light clear and plain, there is nevertheless also in all creature a weak likeness of this perfection of God. Pantheism cannot acknowledge this, but theism confesses that the creature, though absolutely dependent, nevertheless has a proper, distinct being. And in this being the perseverance in its existence is implanted, the conatus in suo esse perseverandi, the Selbsterhaltungstrieb, the instinct for self-preservation. Every creature, insofar as it is, fears death; and even the smallest atom offers resistance to every attempt at annihilation. It is a shadow of the independent, immutable being of our God.


2. From the aseity of God His immutability flows of itself. Yet this seems to find little support in Scripture. There God always stands in the liveliest intercourse with the world. In the beginning He created heaven and earth and thus passed from not-creating to creating. And from that beginning He lives, as it were, the life of the world and especially of His people Israel along with them, He comes and He goes, He reveals and hides Himself, He turns His face away and turns it in grace. He repents, Gen. 6:6, 1 Sam. 15:11, Amos 7:3, 6, Joel 2:13, Jonah 3:9, 4:2, and changes His purpose, Ex. 32:10-14, Jonah 3:10. He becomes angry, Num. 11:1, 10, Ps. 106:40, Zech. 10:3, and lays aside His anger, Deut. 13:17, 2 Chron. 12:12, 30:8, Jer. 18:8, 10, 26:3, 19, 36:3. He is different toward the pious than toward the ungodly, Prov. 11:20, 12:22; with the pure He shows Himself pure but with the crooked He shows Himself a wrestler, Ps. 18:26, 27. In the fullness of time He even becomes man in Christ and comes to dwell in the church by the Holy Spirit; He rejects Israel and receives the Gentiles. And in the life of the children of God there remains a continual alternation of consciousness of guilt and consciousness of forgiveness, of experiences of God's wrath and of His love, of His desertion and of His nearness. But at the same time that same Scripture testifies that God under all that is and remains the same. Everything changes but He remains standing, He remains who He is, Ps. 102:26-28. He is Ihvh, the Being and always remaining like Himself; the first and with the last still the same God, Isa. 41:4, 43:10, 46:4, 48:12. He is who He is, Deut. 32:39, cf. John 8:58, Heb. 13:8, ἀφθαρτος, ὁ μονος ἐχων ἀθανασιαν, altĳd ὁ αἰτος, Rom 1:23, 1 Tim. 1:17, 6:16, Heb. 1:11, 12. And immutable in His existence and essence, He is so also in His thought and will, in all His purposes and decrees. He is not a man that He should lie or repent. What He says, He does, Num. 15:29, 1 Sam. 15:29. His charismata and calling are ἀμεταμελητα, Rom. 11:29. He does not cast away His people, Rom. 11:1. He completes what He began, Ps. 138:8, Phil. 1:6. In one word, He, Ihvh, changes not, Mal. 3:6; with Him is neither variation nor shadow of turning, James 1:17. Upon this the doctrine of the immutabilitas Dei was built in Christian theology. Mythological theogony could not elevate itself to this height, but philosophy named and described God many times as the only, eternal, immutable, unmoved ruler of all things like to Himself, Philolaus e. a., bĳ Zeller, Phil. d. Gr. I⁴ 425, 488 f. II 928. Aristotle concluded from the movement of the world to a primum movens, an αἰδιος οὐσια ἀκινητος, which was one and eternal, necessary, immutable, free from all composition, without any possibility, ὑλη, δυναμις, but only ἐνεργεια, pure εἰδος, pure being, absolute form, το τι ην εἰναι το πρωτον, ib 359-365. Philo called God ἀτρεπτος, ἰσαιτατος ἑαυτῳ, ἀκλινης, παγιος, βεβαιος, ἀμεταβλητος, ἑστως, Dähne, Gesch. Darst. der jüd.-alex. Rel. Philos. I 118. And with this Christian theology agreed. God is, according to Irenaeus, semper idem, sibi aequalis et similis, adv. haer. IV c. 11, cf. Orig. c. Cels. I 21. IV 14. With Augustine the immutability of God flowed directly from this, that He was the highest, perfect being. Aan alle redelĳk schepsel is het naturaliter et veraciter insitum, esse omnino incommutabilem et incorruptibilem Deum, de Gen. ad litt. VII 11. Through the senses this concept of an eternal and immutable being cannot be obtained, for every creature, also man himself, is mutable; but within himself he sees and finds that immutable, which is better and greater than all that changes, de lib. arb. II 6, de doctr. chr. I 9. If God were not immutable, He would not be God, Conf. VII 4. His name is _being_ and this name is a nomen incommutabilitatis. All that changes ceases to be what it was. But the true being is only the portion of Him who does not change. What truly is, that remains. Quod est, manet. But what changes fuit aliquid et aliquid erit; non tamen est, quia mutabile est, de trin. V 2. God, however, who is, cannot change, for every change would be a diminution of His being. And as immutable as God is in His essence, He is so also in His knowing, willing, and decreeing. Dei essentia, qua est, nihil mutabile habet, nec in aeternitate, nec in veritate, nec in voluntate, de trin. IV prooem. As He is, so He knows and wills, in immutable manner. Essentia tua scit et vult incommutabiliter et scientia tua est et vult incommutabiliter et tua voluntas est et scit incommutabiliter, Conf. XIII, 16. Creation, revelation, incarnation, affections, etc., brought no change in God. In God there never came a novum consilium, de ord. II 17. There has always been in God one immutable will. Una eademque sempiterna et immutabili voluntate res quas condidit, et ut prius non essent egit, quamdiu non fuerunt, et ut posterius essent, quando esse coeperunt, de civ. XII, 17. Only there is change in the creature from non-being to being, from good to evil, etc. The same thought we find then later repeatedly back, with John Damascene, de fide orthod. I c. 8. Thomas, S. Theol. I qu 9. Lombardus, Sent. I dist. 8, 3. Bonaventura, Sent. I dist. 8 art. 2 qu 1-2. Petavius, de Deo III c. 1-2. and so also with Lutheran and Reformed theologians, Gerhard, Loc. II cap. 8 sect. 5. Zanchius, Op. II col. 77-83. Polanus, Synt. theol. II c. 13, cf. Conf. Gall. art. 1. Conf. Belg. 1 enz.

But this immutability of God found much opposition, from both deistic and pantheistic side. With Epicurus the gods are entirely like excellent men, who change place, activity, thought, etc., Zeller IV 430 f., and with the Stoa the Deity was, as earlier with Heraclitus, taken up as the immanent cause of the world in its ceaseless change, ib. IV 133 f. Of the same nature was the opposition to the immutability of God in Christian theology. On one side stand Pelagianism, Socinianism, Remonstrantism, which especially opposes the immutability of God's knowing and willing, and makes the will of God dependent upon and thus lets it change according to the conduct of man. Particularly Vorstius in his work de Deo et ejus attributis opposed the immutability of God. He distinguished between the essentia of God, which is simple and immutable, and the voluntas Dei, which as libera also does not eternally and not always will the same, Dorner, Gesammelte Schriften 278. But much more serious is the opposition to the immutability of God from the pantheistic side. Common to this opposition is that the idea of becoming is transferred to God and thus the boundary between Creator and creature is completely erased. The idea of God as substantia, as it occurs with Spinoza, proved to be an abstract concept without content. To bring life into that concept, philosophy often replaced being by becoming. Naturally there is still great difference there, according as one conceives this process by which God Himself becomes unitarianly or trinitarianly and lets it take place immanent in the essence of God or transient in the world. To this belongs in the first place Gnosticism, but then further the theosophy of the Kabbalah, of Böhme, Baader, Schelling, Rothe, Hamberger, etc., continuing to work in the doctrine of kenosis, and finally the pantheistic philosophy of Fichte, Hegel, Schleiermacher, Schopenhauer, Hartmann, etc. However differently elaborated, the fundamental thought is one. God is not but becomes. An sich, in the first moment, He is βυθος ἀγνωστος, an abstract purely potential being, nature without more, contentless thinking, a dark urge, a blind alogical will, in one word, a being that is nothing but can become all. But out of that potential being God brings Himself in the form of a process gradually to actuality. He is His own Creator. He produces Himself. He comes gradually, whether in Himself or in the world, to personality, to self-consciousness, to spirit. God is causa sui. Under the influence of this philosophical idea of the absolute becoming the immutability of God has also in more recent theology been denied or limited several times, and God with preference called causa sui, self-actualizing power, Rothe, Theol. Ethik § 16-38. Martensen, Chr. Dogm. 1856 S. 86. 116, cf. Chr. Ethik. I 90 f. Luthardt says, Gott ist seine eigene That, Apol. Vortr. über die Grundwahrh. des Chr. 1870 I 45, cf. Müller, Die chr. Lehre v. d. Sünde II⁵ 171 f. Others speak of a Selbstsetzung Gottes, Dorner, Chr. Gl. I 244. 245. Frank, Syst. der chr. Wahrh. I² 116 f. In his treatise Ueber die richtige Fassung des dogm. Begriffs der Unveränderlichkeit Gottes, Ges. Schriften 1883 S. 188-377, Dorner attempts to avoid both deism and pantheism (acosmism) and to unite the immutability and the vitality of God with one another. He thinks to reach this goal by positing the immutability of God in the ethical, S. 340 f. Ethically God is immutable and always like Himself, He remains holy love, S. 352. But otherwise Dorner thinks that through creation, incarnation, and satisfaction, etc., change has indeed come in God, S. 309. 311 f. 358 f. 364, that He stands in Wechselverhältniss to man, S. 319. 362, that He knows reality only from the world, S. 320, that there is also for Him a past, present, and future, S. 322, that He is angry, justifies, and has a disposition corresponding to man, S. 324 enz. And essentially so also Weisse, Philos. Dogm. I 573. F. A. B. Nitzsch, Ev. Dogm. 365 e. a. Many also in the doctrine of God do not express themselves on this important property, but let it first be noticed at creation or at incarnation or at kenosis, etc., that they assume change in God, as for example Ebrard, Hofmann, Thomasius, etc.

Nevertheless the doctrine of the immutability of God is of the highest importance for religion. The distinction between Creator and creature lies in the opposition between being and becoming. All that is creature is becoming. It is mutable, strives continually, seeks for rest and satisfaction and finds that rest only in Him who is pure being without becoming. Therefore God is called in Scripture so often the rock, Deut. 32:4, 15, 18, 30, 31, 37. 1 Sam. 2:2, 2 Sam. 22:3, 32, Ps. 19:15, 31:3, 62:3, 7, 73:26 enz. Upon Him man can rely, He changes not in His essence, nor in His knowing or willing, He remains who He is eternally. Every change is foreign to God. There is in Him no change of time, for He is eternal; nor of place for He is omnipresent; nor also of essence for He is pure being. Christian theology also expressed this latter many times by the term purus actus. Aristotle had thus conceived the essence of God, as πρωτον εἰδος without any δυναμις, as absolute ἐνεργεια. Scholasticism therefore proceeded to speak of God as actus purissimus et simplicissimus, to indicate that He is the perfect, absolute being without any potentia to non-being or to other-being. Thus Boethius says, de cons. philos. lib. III, that God does not change of essence (forma), quia purus actus est, cf. Thomas, S. Theol. I qu. 3. Therefore the expression causa sui was also avoided of God. The idea of the absolute becoming was first clearly expressed by Heraclitus and thereafter repeatedly returned in philosophy. Especially Plotinus makes use of this concept, not only in application to matter, but also to that which he holds to be the absolute being. He teaches concerning God, that He has produced His own being, that He was active before He was, Thilo, Kurze pragm. Gesch. d. Philos. I 352 f. Now Christian theology certainly spoke of God that He was an ens a se, and thus of His aseitas. And also Lactantius, Synesius, and Hieronymus employed the expression causa sui; the latter wrote: Deus qui semper est nec habet aliunde principium et ipse sui origo est suaeque causa substantiae, non potest intelligi aliunde habere quod subsistit, Comm. ad Eph. cap. 3:15. But this expression was nevertheless always understood so, that God indeed existed from Himself but not that He had become and been produced from Himself, Klee, Kath. Dogm. II 43. When later Descartes accorded priority to the will of God above the intellect and let the essentia of all things depend on that will, indeed even let God's existence be a product of His will, so that he said, Deus se revera conservat, God is causa sui en a se, niet in negatieven maar in positieven zin, God is causa efficiens sui ipsius
