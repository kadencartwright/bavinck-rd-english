§ 27. The Personal Names of God.

1. Even higher than in the names of being does God’s revelation ascend in the nomina personalia, which make known to us the distinctions that exist in the unity of His being. This revelation already begins in the O.T. To be sure, it does not yet appear there fully, as the Church Fathers and later theologians often taught, disregarding the historical character of the revelation; but neither is it correct that it would not be found there at all, as was taught after the Socinians and the Remonstrants by Semler, Herder, Doederlein, Bretschneider, Dogm. I⁴ 565 f., Hofmann, Schriftbeweis I² 90 f., et al., cf. Walch, Bibl. theol. sel. II 687. The O.T. makes known the Trinitarian existence of God only obscurely; it is the document of the emerging doctrine of the Trinity, Petavius, de trin. II c. 7. Lange, Dogm. II 124 f., 148 f. But it does contain, not only in isolated texts standing by themselves but especially in the organism of its revelation, moments that are of the highest significance for the doctrine of the Trinity. First, the name Elohim comes into consideration. That this in its plural form is no proof for the Trinity was already observed earlier, p. 104. But still, it is remarkable that this name never met with objection from the advocates of monotheism on account of its form. This is to be explained only from the fact that it contains no reminiscence of polytheism, but indicates the Godhead in her fullness and richness of life. The God of revelation is no abstract unity, but the living, true God, who includes the highest diversity in the infinite fullness of His life. Already immediately in creation this comes forth. Elohim creates by speaking and by the sending forth of His Spirit. The word that God speaks is no sound, but a power, so great that thereby He creates and sustains the world; He speaks and it is there, Gen. 1:3, Ps. 33:6, 9, 147:18, 148:8, Joel 2:11. That word spoken by God, proceeding from Him and thus distinct from Him, is later hypostatized as wisdom in Job 28:23–27, Prov. 8:22 ff., cf. Prov. 3:19, Jer. 10:12, 51:15. That wisdom is from eternity possessed, prepared, appointed, sought out by God, as His nursling and master workwoman, through whom He created and sustains all things. But not only through the word and wisdom, but also through the Spirit of God does the work of creation and sustaining come about, Gen. 1:2, Ps. 33:6, 104:33, 139:7, Job 26:13, 27:3, 32:8, 33:4; Isa. 40:7, 13, 59:19. While the word is the mediator through whom God calls all things into existence, it is His Spirit through whom He is immanent in all the created, and makes everything living and adorns it. Thus it comes forth already according to the teaching of the O.T. in creation that all things owe their origin and continued existence to a threefold causa. Elohim and κόσμος do not stand dualistically alongside one another, but the world, created by God, has His Word as its objective, His Spirit as its subjective principle. The world is first thought by God and therefore comes into being through His almighty speaking, and when it has received reality, it does not stand outside and over against Him but remains resting in His Spirit. Even more clearly does this threefold cause come forth in the O.T. in the realm of special revelation, in the work of re-creation. Then it is no longer Elohim alone, but Yahweh who reveals Himself, who makes Himself known as the God of the covenant and of the oath, of revelation and of history. But even as such He does not reveal Himself directly and immediately, Ex. 33:20. It is again through His Word that He makes Himself known and saves and preserves His people, Ps. 107:20. And the bearer of that word of the revelation of salvation is the Malak Ihvh, the Messenger of the Covenant. Not always, where the expression Angel of God or Angel of the Lord occurs in the O.T., is one to think of the angelus increatus, as Hengstenberg believed. In 2 Sam. 24:16 ff., 1 Kgs 19:5–7, 2 Kgs 19:35, Dan. 3:25, 28, 6:23, 10:13, we are to think of an ordinary angel, just as also in Matt. 1:20, 28, Luke 1:11, 2:9, Acts 5:19, 8:26, 10:3, 12:7, 23, 27:23, Jude 9, Rev. 12:7. Concerning other places doubt can exist, such as Num. 22:22 ff., Josh. 5:13–14, Judg. 2:1–14, 6:11–24, 13:2–23. But in the places mentioned Part I 250, the subject who speaks and acts in the Angel of the Lord far exceeds a created angel. The Church Fathers before Augustine saw unanimously in this Angel of the Lord a theophany of the Logos, cf. the quotations in Trip, Die Theophanien in den Geschichtsbüchern des A. T., Leiden 1858, S. 20–41. Often, however, this view was connected with the opinion that the Father is properly invisible, inaccessible, ineffable, but that the Son can reveal Himself and is the principle of all revelation; thus with Justin Martyr, Theophilus, Irenaeus, Tertullian. But this separation and opposition between the Father and the Son was rightly contested by the later Church Fathers, Athanasius, the three Cappadocians, etc. The Son was truly God and thus just as invisible as the Father. Thus the view of Augustine was prepared, who also thought the theophanies of God in the O.T. always mediated by created angels, de trin. III 11, de civ. XVI c. 29. The scholastic and Roman theologians usually adopted this exegesis of Augustine, Thomas, S. Theol. I qu. 51 art. 2 and 3 ad 5. I 2 qu. 98 art. 3. Sent. II dist. 8 qu. 1 art. 6. Petavius, de trin. VIII c. 2. Scheeben, Kath. Dogm. I 784 f. C. Pesch, Prael. dogm. II 245, etc. Luther and Calvin thought at one time of a created, then of an uncreated angel, Trip, 49–58; but the later Protestant interpreters understood those passages mostly of the Logos, especially also in opposition to the Socinians, Remonstrants, and Rationalists, who saw nothing but angelophanies in them. While Hofmann, Baumgarten, Delitzsch join the latter view, the old view has again been defended by Stier, Hengstenberg, Keil, Kurtz, Ebrard, etc., also Philippi, Kirchl. Glaub. II³ 191–196. The difference between these two interpretations is not as great as it seems. The advocates of the ancient ecclesiastical view must after all acknowledge that the Logos has assumed a human form; and Augustine and his followers must concede that in that created angel the Logos revealed Himself in a very particular way, Rivetus in Trip 65. And thereto comes that the places where mention is made of the Angel of the Lord cannot all be understood in the same sense. This much, then, is certain, that in the Malak Ihvh, who preeminently bears that name, God and then specifically the Logos was present in a wholly unique way. That appears clearly from the fact that He, although distinct from Yahweh, is yet also one with Him in name, in power, in redemption and blessing, in worship and honor, Part I 250. Trip, loc. cit., 100 f. This exegesis is moreover recommended by the whole of O.T. and N.T. Scripture, Job 33:23, Ps. 34:8, 35:5, Prov. 8:22 ff., 30:4, Isa. 9:5, Hos. 12:5–6, Mic. 5:6, Zech. 1:8–14, 3:1 ff., 12:8, Mal. 3:1, John 8:56, 58, cf. John 1:1–5, 1 Cor. 10:4, 9; and Acts 7:30, 35, 38, Gal. 3:19, Heb. 2:2 are not in conflict with this. And as Yahweh now objectively reveals Himself in the re-creation through His word, in the Malak Ihvh; so He does this subjectively in and through His Spirit. The Spirit of God is the principle of all life and salvation, of all gifts and powers within the realm of revelation; of courage, Judg. 3:10, 6:34, 11:29, 13:25, 1 Sam. 11:6; of bodily strength, Judg. 14:6, 15:14; of skill, Ex. 28:3, 31:3–5, 35:31–35, 1 Chr. 28:12; of statesmanship, Num. 11:17, 25, 1 Sam. 16:13; of understanding and wisdom, Job 32:8, Isa. 11:2; of holiness and renewal, Ps. 51:13, Isa. 63:10, cf. Gen. 6:3, Neh. 9:20, 1 Sam. 10:6, 9; of prophecy and prediction, Num. 11:25, 29, 24:2–3, Mic. 3:8, etc. In a special manner He will rest upon the Messiah, Isa. 11:2, 42:1, 61:1, but thereafter also be poured out upon all flesh, Joel 2:28–29, Isa. 32:15, 44:3, Ezek. 36:26–27, 39:29, Zech. 12:10, and give all a new heart and a new spirit, Ezek. 36:26–27. Concerning the Holy Spirit in the O.T.: Kleinert, zur altt. Lehre vom Geiste Gottes, Jahrb. f. d. Th. 1867 S. 3–60. Oehler, Theol. des A. T.² 178 f., 214 f. Smend, Altt. Religionsgesch. 459 f. Warfield, The Spirit of God in the Old Test., Presb. and Ref. Rev. Oct. 1895 p. 665–687. This threefold Divine principle, which underlies creation and re-creation, and bears the whole oeconomy of O.T. revelation, is now also mentioned together several times. The threefold repetitions in Dan. 9:19, Zech. 1:3, Isa. 6:3, 33:22 do not come into consideration for this; only the high-priestly benediction, Num. 6:24–26, points back in the threefold character of its blessing to a threefold revelation of God and is thus the O.T. example of the apostolic blessing, 2 Cor. 13:13. The plural forms in Gen. 1:26–27, 3:22, Isa. 6:8, etc., lack sufficient force, because they are to be explained in the same way as the pluralis Elohim, cf. above p. 104. Of more significance are places such as Gen. 19:24, Ps. 45:8, 110:1, Hos. 1:7, because they point to a self-distinction in the Divine being. And a threefold self-distinction in the Divine being is indicated most clearly in Ps. 33:6, Isa. 61:1, 63:9–12, Hag. 2:5–6. Many formerly also saw in the three men who appeared to Abraham, Gen. 18, a revelation of the Trinity, e.g., Witsius, Oec. foed. IV 3 § 3–8. Others thought that one of the three was the Logos and the other two were ordinary angels, e.g., Calvin, Kantt. Statenvert., Moor I 807. Much more plausible, however, is the exegesis of Augustine, de civ. XVI 29, according to whom the three men were three created angels, in whom, however, Yahweh in a special way revealed Himself and was present. Lit. concerning the Trinity in the O.T. in M. Vitringa I 213 sq., 218 sq.

2. These thoughts of the O.T. have been fruitful in various directions. First, they have been adopted and further elaborated in the apocryphal literature. In the Wisdom of Jesus Sirach, wisdom takes a broad place. She is from God and created by Him before all things, and she remains with Him unto eternity. She is spread out over all God’s works, but has her resting place especially in Zion and is to be found in the law, ch. 1:1–40 and ch. 24, cf. also Baruch, ch. 3:9–4:4. But the Book of Wisdom goes further, 6:22–9. Here wisdom is so hypostatized that Divine attributes and works are ascribed to her. She is clearly distinguished from God, for she is the breath of His power, an effusion of His glory, a reflection of His light, 7:25–26. But she is also most intimately connected with God, shares life with Him, is initiated into His knowledge and chooses the ideas which shall be executed, 8:3–4. She is the παρεδρος at His throne, knows all His works and was present at the creation of the world, 9:4, 9. Yes, she is herself the one who creates, rules, and renews all things, 7:27, 8:1, 5. She is identical with God’s Word, 9:1–2, cf. 16:12, 18:15–16, and with His Spirit, 1:4–7, 9:17, 12:1. Already in this Book of Wisdom the influence of Greek philosophy is noticeable, especially 7:22 ff., but this is much more the case with Philo. The relation of God to the world was already investigated by Plato. From the distinction and opposition of δόξα and ἐπιστήμη Plato deduced that, just as the former has a sensible object in reality, so the latter must have an eternal and immutable being, i.e., an idea, as its object. These ideas, although properly nothing other than general concepts, were elevated by Plato to metaphysical principia, to independent substances, to a sort of intermediate beings, after which the δημιουργός formed the κόσμος and which are thus the παραδείγματα and the αἰτίαι of things. Although Aristotle subjected this theory of ideas to a sharp critique, yet the thought that an intelligent, spiritual principle underlies all things was never again lost from Greek philosophy. Especially the Stoa placed emphasis upon the fact that a Divine reason was the ground of all phenomena. It employed for this the name λόγος σπερματικός, because all being and life germinates from that logos as from a seed, and also spoke sometimes in plurali of λόγοι σπερματικοί, in order therewith to indicate besides the unity also the diversity of the all-creating natural power, Zeller, Philos. d. Gr. IV³ 159, 160, cf. 140 n. Even the later so significant distinction between λόγος ἐνδιάθετος and προφορικός is borrowed from the Stoa, ibid. 67 n. This Greek doctrine of the idea, the νοῦς and the λόγος had already before Philo been connected with the doctrine of the O.T. concerning the word and wisdom, Zeller, ibid. V 266. But yet it was especially Philo who fused all these different elements—Platonic theory of ideas, Stoic doctrine of the logos, O.T. doctrine of wisdom, etc.—into one system. He proceeds therein from the dualism of God and world. God is ἄποιος, indescribable; we can only say that He is, not what He is. Therefore He cannot also stand in immediate contact with matter. Before God created the sensible world, He made a plan and formed in His thought the κόσμος νοητός, the ideas, as παραδείγματα and δυνάμεις of all things. These ideas are now presented by Philo as the powers through which God can work in the world, and described now more now less figuratively, now more now less personally, as servants, governors, envoys, mediators, as λόγοι and powers, as bonds and pillars, which by Moses were called angels and by the Greeks δαίμονες, as thoughts which are found in the Divine thinking and are uncreated and infinite as God Himself. These ideas are now also many in number, but they find their unity in the logos, the idea that contains all ideas, the power that contains all powers, the book that contains all thoughts in itself, the κόσμος νοητός itself. And in the same way as the Divine ideas, so also this logos is described, at one time more as an attribute of God and one with His wisdom, and then more as a being distinct from God. He stands, as it were, between God and world and has part in both; He is not uncreated as God, and yet also not created as finite things; He is deputy, envoy, interpreter, governor, angel, instrument, image, shadow of God, yea His firstborn Son, His eldest Son in distinction from the world which is His youngest son; Philo calls him even God, but δεύτερος θεός. Zeller places clearly in the light that the logos in Philo, just as the Divine ideas, bears and must bear this double character. He is an intermediate being and therefore a double being, an attribute of God and yet a person, neither identical with God nor also a creature as the world, an idea in the thinking of God and a power in the world, hovering back and forth between an impersonal attribute and a personal subsistence, but therefore precisely considered suitable as mediator between God and the world, cf. Zeller, Philos. d. Gr. V³ 370–386, and lit. concerning Philo there 338, with also Max Heinze, Die Lehre v. Logos in der gr. Philos. 1872. Schürer, Neut. Zeitgesch. 653 f. Meijer-Weiss, Ev. In Jewish theology this doctrine of intermediate beings has been developed further. As completely transcendent, God cannot immediately enter into connection with creatures. For this He requires all kinds of intermediate beings. When He wishes to do nothing other than guide the present powers in nature and humanity, then He employs angels. But if He wishes to work in the world creatively or recreatively, hypostases appear, which, although being creatures, yet bear Divine attributes, because they are representatives of God. Such hypostases are Metraton, the μεταθρόνιος of God; Memra, the word of God; Schechina, the presence of God’s glory; Bath Kol, the voice of God, which gives revelations and proclaims oracles; Ruach hakkodesch, the Spirit who proceeds from God and communicates higher knowledge, Eisenmenger, Entdecktes Judenthum, I 265 f., II 393 f., etc. Weber, System der altsyn. paläst. Theol. 172–189. Herzog² 2, 230 f., 13, 458 f.

3. Although the Wisdom of Jesus Sirach still joins itself quite closely to the canonical literature, in the Book of Wisdom, with Philo, and in Jewish theology an influence of philosophy is to be perceived, which causes them to deviate increasingly from the thoughts of the O.T. First, there is a difference in principle. The doctrine of intermediate beings in Philo and later Jewish theology is born from the Platonic opposition of God and world, of which no trace is yet to be noticed in the O.T. books. The word and wisdom in the O.T. are no mediating intermediate beings between God and world, but they stand wholly and entirely on the side of God, belong to Him, and are the principia of the created world. In Philo, however, the intermediate beings assume an impossible place: they are neither God nor creature, neither person nor attribute, neither subsistence nor power, but have part in both; they erase the boundary line which in the O.T. always separates the creature from the Creator and prepare the way for the philosophy of Gnosticism and the Kabbalah. In the second place, there is a difference no less great in the character which the doctrine of the word and wisdom in Scripture and which the doctrine of intermediate beings in Philo bears. In Philo the logos in its first meaning is identical with reason, the thinking, the thought in God and thus as such immanent in God, nothing but an attribute. Only in its second meaning does that logos become word, which proceeds from God and occupies a mediating place between God and world. In the O.T., however, the word is not first reason and thought of God, much less an ideal world-image, a κόσμος νοητός, but the spoken word through which He creates and sustains all things. And also wisdom in Job and Proverbs is presented not as an attribute of God, but personally as possessed and ordained by God from eternity and consulted and searched out at the creation of all things. Third, the intermediate beings in Philo and the Jewish theologians have no soteriological significance. To be sure, they serve for enlightenment and for the communication of knowledge, but of a connection between those intermediate beings and the Messiah there is no question. They even push the doctrine of the Messiah as revelation of the truth and as author of salvation into the background. And now it is indeed true that also in the O.T. the connection between word and wisdom, Servant of God and Messiah, Malak Ihvh and Davidide, is not yet clearly brought to completion. The lines still run parallel to one another. But they nevertheless approach one another. Elohim and Yahweh is the same God. He who as Elohim creates and sustains the world through Word and Spirit, is the same who as Yahweh has led Israel through His Malak, will save His people through the Servant of Yahweh, and will rule them eternally through the Messiah from David’s house, and renew and sanctify them all through His Spirit. And these lines, which in the O.T. increasingly approach one another, culminate in Him who is the Logos, the Prophet, Priest and King, in whom God comes to His people and dwells eternally among them. For Philo an incarnation of the logos would have been an absurd thought. In the N.T. this is precisely the highest revelation of God. Finally, it may here yet be added that the doctrine of intermediate beings in Philo, etc., is not finished and has no limit. Philo certainly brings some unity into the Divine ideas by summarizing them in the logos, but the same thing which he says of this one, he also repeatedly speaks of all Divine ideas. In Jewish theology the number of intermediate beings increases more and more. It is an emanation, as with the αἰῶνες in Gnosticism. The dualism avenges itself to the very end. The intermediate beings bring about no communion between God and world, because they actually are neither of the two. The world nevertheless always remains outside and over against God. The significance of the Spirit of God is not recognized. In the O.T. this doctrine of the Spirit occupies a great place. In the apocryphal literature, with Philo, and in Jewish theology it is almost entirely neglected. The Spirit is at most only a Spirit of prediction, granted to a few, Zeller, V 384, Weber, 184 f., but no longer the Spirit of the O.T., who closes and completes creation and re-creation. For all these reasons there is a principal distinction between the development which the Trinitarian thoughts of the O.T. have found in the apocryphal literature, with Philo and the Jews, and that which is given to it in the N.T. Although the N.T. may have some words in common with Philo, etc., and also speak of Christ as λόγος, εἰκών, ἀπαύγασμα, υἱός, θεός, etc.; the agreement does not go further. The N.T. is written in Greek, in the κοινὴ διάλεκτος, in the language which existed and was spoken everywhere. It created no new language. The thoughts of God have assumed the σάρξ of human language. But in those words God has placed a new content. There is agreement in form, the content differs. Philo and John have no more than the name of Logos in common.
