§ 27. THE PERSONAL NAMES OF GOD.

1. Higher still than in the names of essence, God’s revelation ascends in the nomina personalia, which make known to us the distinctions that exist in the unity of His being. This revelation already begins in the O.T. To be sure, it does not yet appear there in full, as the church fathers and later theologians often taught with disregard for the historical character of the revelation; but it is equally incorrect that it would not yet be found there at all, as was taught after the Socinians and the Remonstrants by Semler, Herder, Doederlein, Bretschneider, Dogm. I⁴ 565 f., Hofmann, Schriftbeweis I² 90 f., et al., cf. Walch, Bibl. theol. sel. II 687. The O.T. makes the Trinitarian existence of God known only obscurely; it is the charter of the developing doctrine of the Trinity, Petavius, de trin. II c. 7; Lange, Dogm. II 124 f., 148 f. But it nevertheless contains, not in a few isolated texts alone but especially in the organism of its revelation, moments that are of the highest significance for the doctrine of the Trinity. First, the name Elohim comes into consideration. That this in its plural form is no proof for the Trinity was already noted earlier, p. 104. But it is nevertheless remarkable that this name, on account of its form, never met with objection among the advocates of monotheism. This is to be explained only from the fact that it contains no reminiscence of polytheism, but denotes the Godhead in her fullness and richness of life. The God of revelation is no abstract unity, but the living, true God, who in the infinite fullness of His life includes the highest diversity. Already immediately at creation this comes forth. Elohim creates by speaking and by the sending forth of His Spirit. The word that God speaks is no sound, but a power, so great that thereby He creates and sustains the world; He speaks and it is there, Gen. 1:3, Ps. 33:6, 9, 147:18, 148:8, Joel 2:11. That word spoken by God, proceeding from Him and thus distinguished from Him, is later hypostasized as wisdom in Job 28:23-27, Prov. 8:22 ff., cf. Prov. 3:19, Jer. 10:12, 51:15. This wisdom is from eternity possessed, prepared, appointed, searched out by God, as His nursling and master-workwoman, through whom He created and sustains all things. But not only by the word and the wisdom, but also by the Spirit of God does the work of creation and sustenance come about, Gen. 1:2, Ps. 33:6, 104:33, 139:7, Job 26:13, 27:3, 32:8, 33:4; Isa. 40:7, 13, 59:19. While the word is the mediator through whom God calls all things into existence, it is His Spirit through whom He is immanent in all created things, and makes all things alive and adorns them. Thus it appears already according to the teaching of the O.T. in creation, that all things owe their origin and continuance to a threefold causa. Elohim and kosmos do not stand dualistically beside each other, but the world, created by God, has His Word as its objective, His Spirit as its subjective principle. The world is first thought by God and therefore comes into existence through His almighty speaking, and when it has received reality, it does not stand outside and over against Him but remains resting in His Spirit. Even more clearly does this threefold cause appear in the O.T. in the domain of special revelation, in the work of re-creation. Then it is no longer Elohim alone, but Jahveh who reveals Himself, who makes Himself known as the God of the covenant and of the oath, of revelation and of history. But even as such He does not reveal Himself directly and immediately, Ex. 33:20. It is again through His Word that He makes Himself known and saves and preserves His people, Ps. 107:20. And the bearer of that word of salvation-revelation is the Malak Ihvh, the Messenger of the Covenant. Not always, where the expression Angel of God or Angel of the Lord occurs in the O.T., is the angelus increatus to be thought of, as Hengstenberg supposed. In 2 Sam. 24:16 ff., 1 Kings 19:5-7, 2 Kings 19:35, Dan. 3:25, 28, 6:23, 10:13, we have to think of an ordinary angel, just as also in Matt. 1:20, 28, Luke 1:11, 2:9, Acts 5:19, 8:26, 10:3, 12:7, 23, 27:23, Jude 9, Rev. 12:7. About other places doubt may exist, such as Num. 22:22 ff., Josh. 5:13, 14, Judg. 2:1-14, 6:11-24, 13:2-23. But in the places mentioned in part I 250, the subject who speaks and acts in the angel of the Lord far exceeds a created angel. The church fathers before Augustine saw unanimously in this Angel of the Lord a theophany of the Logos, cf. the quotations in Trip, Die Theophanien in den Geschichtsbüchern des A. T. Leiden 1858 S. 20-41. Often this view was however connected with the opinion that the Father is essentially invisible, inaccessible, ineffable, but that the Son can reveal Himself and is the principle of all revelation; thus in Justin Martyr, Theophilus, Irenaeus, Tertullian. But this separation and contrast between the Father and the Son was rightly contested by the later church fathers, Athanasius, the three Cappadocians, etc. The Son was truly God and thus equally invisible as the Father. Thus the view of Augustine was prepared, who also thought that the theophanies of God in the O.T. were always mediated by created angels, de trin. III 11, de civ. XVI c. 29. The scholastic and Roman theologians usually adopted this exegesis of Augustine, Thomas, S. Theol. I qu. 51 art. 2 and 3 ad 5; I 2 qu. 98 art. 3; Sent. II dist. 8 qu. 1 art. 6; Petavius, de trin. VIII c. 2; Scheeben, Kath. Dogm. I 784 f.; C. Pesch, Prael. dogm. II 245, etc. Luther and Calvin sometimes thought of a created, then of the uncreated angel, Trip, 49-58; but later Protestant interpreters understood those passages mostly of the Logos, especially also in contrast to the Socinians, Remonstrants, and Rationalists, who saw nothing but angelophanies in them. While Hofmann, Baumgarten, Delitzsch join themselves to the latter view, the old view has again been defended by Stier, Hengstenberg, Keil, Kurtz, Ebrard, etc., also Philippi, Kirchl. Glaub. II³ 191-196. The difference between these two interpretations is not as great as it appears. The advocates of the ancient ecclesiastical view must after all acknowledge that the Logos has assumed a human form; and Augustine and his followers must concede that in that created angel the Logos revealed Himself in a most particular way, Rivetus in Trip 65. And besides, the places where the angel of the Lord is spoken of cannot all be understood in the same sense. So much is therefore certain, that in the Malak Ihvh, who pre-eminently bears that name, God and then specifically the Logos was present in a wholly unique way. That appears clearly from the fact that He, though distinguished from Jehovah, is nevertheless also one with Him in name, in power, in redemption and blessing, in worship and honor, part I 250; Trip, t. a. p. 100 f. This exegesis is moreover recommended by the whole of O. and N.T. Scripture, Job 33:23, Ps. 34:8, 35:5, Prov. 8:22 ff., 30:4, Isa. 9:5, Hos. 12:5, 6, Mic. 5:6, Zech. 1:8-14, 3:1 ff., 12:8, Mal. 3:1, John 8:56, 58, cf. John 1:1-5, 1 Cor. 10:4, 9; and Acts 7:30, 35, 38, Gal. 3:19, Heb. 2:2 are not in conflict with this. And just as Jahveh now objectively reveals Himself in re-creation through His word, in the Malak Ihvh; so does He do this subjectively in and through His Spirit. The Spirit of God is the principle of all life and salvation, of all gifts and powers within the domain of revelation; of courage, Judg. 3:10, 6:34, 11:29, 13:25, 1 Sam. 11:6; of physical strength, Judg. 14:6, 15:14; of artistic skill, Ex. 28:3, 31:3-5, 35:31-35, 1 Chr. 28:12; of government, Num. 11:17, 25, 1 Sam. 16:13; of understanding and wisdom, Job 32:8, Isa. 11:2; of holiness and renewal, Ps. 51:13, Isa. 63:10, cf. Gen. 6:3, Neh. 9:20, 1 Sam. 10:6, 9; of prophecy and prediction, Num. 11:25, 29, 24:2, 3, Mic. 3:8, etc. In a particular manner He will rest upon the Messiah, Isa. 11:2, 42:1, 61:1, but thereafter also be poured out upon all flesh, Joel 3:1, 2, Isa. 32:15, 44:3, Ezek. 36:26, 27, 39:29, Zech. 12:10, and give to all a new heart and a new spirit, Ezek. 36:26, 27. On the Holy Spirit in the O.T.: Kleinert, zur altt. Lehre vom Geiste Gottes, Jahrb. f. d. Th. 1867 S. 3-60; Oehler, Theol. des A. T.² 178 f., 214 f.; Smend, Altt. Religionsgesch. 459 f.; Warfield, The Spirit of God in the Old Test., Presb. and Ref. Rev. Oct. 1895 p. 665-687. This threefold divine principle, which lies at the foundation of creation and re-creation, and bears the whole oeconomy of O.T. revelation, is now also mentioned together a few times. The threefold repetitions in Dan. 9:19, Zech. 1:3, Isa. 6:3, 33:22 do not come into consideration for this; only the priestly blessing, Num. 6:24-26, points in the threefold character of its blessing to a threefold revelation of God and is thus the O.T. example of the apostolic benediction, 2 Cor. 13:13. The plural forms in Gen. 1:26, 27, 3:22, Isa. 6:8, etc. lack sufficient force, because they are to be explained in the same manner as the pluralis Elohim, cf. above p. 104. Of more significance are passages such as Gen. 19:24, Ps. 45:8, 110:1, Hos. 1:7, because they point to a self-distinction in the divine being. And most clearly a threefold self-distinction in the divine being is indicated in Ps. 33:6, Isa. 61:1, 63:9-12, Hag. 2:5, 6. Many have formerly also seen in the three men who appeared to Abraham, Gen. 18, a revelation of the Trinity, e.g., Witsius, Oec. foed. IV 3 § 3-8. Others opined that one of the three was the Logos and the other two ordinary angels, e.g., Calvin, Kantt. Statenvert., Moor I 807. Much more probable, however, is the exegesis of Augustine, de civ. XVI 29, according to whom the three men were three created angels, in whom Jahveh nevertheless revealed Himself and was present in a special way. Literature on the Trinity in the O.T. in M. Vitringa I 213 sq., 218 sq.

2. These thoughts of the O.T. have been fruitful in many directions. First, they have been adopted and further developed in the apocryphal literature. In the Wisdom of Jesus Sirach, wisdom occupies a broad place. She is from God and before all things created by Him, and she remains with Him to eternity. She is spread out over all God’s works, but has her resting place especially in Zion and is to be found in the law, c. 1:1-40 and cap. 24, cf. also Baruch, c. 3:9-4:4. But the Book of Wisdom goes further, 6:22-9. Here wisdom is so hypostasized that divine attributes and works are ascribed to her. She is clearly distinguished from God, for she is the breath of His power, an emanation of His glory, a reflection of His light, 7:25, 26. But she is also most intimately connected with God, lives with Him, is initiated into His knowledge and chooses those ideas which shall be executed, 8:3, 4. She is the παρεδρος at His throne, knows all His works and was present at the creation of the world, 9:4, 9. Indeed, she is herself the one who creates, rules, and renews all things, 7:27, 8:1, 5. She is identical with God’s Word, 9:1, 2, cf. 16:12, 18:15, 16, and with His Spirit, 1:4-7, 9:17, 12:1. Already in this Book of Wisdom the influence of Greek philosophy is noticeable, especially 7:22 ff., but much stronger is this the case with Philo. The relation of God to the world was already investigated by Plato. From the distinction and contrast of δοξα and ἐπιστημη Plato deduced that, just as the former has a sensible object in reality, so the latter must have an eternal and immutable being, i.e., an idea, as its object. These ideas, though essentially nothing other than general concepts, were elevated by Plato to metaphysical principia, to independent substances, to a sort of intermediate beings, according to which the δημιουργος formed the kosmos and which thus are the παραδειγματα and the αἰτιαι of things. Although Aristotle subjected this doctrine of ideas to a sharp critique, the thought that an intelligent, spiritual principle lay at the foundation of all things has nevertheless not been lost from Greek philosophy. Especially the Stoa emphasized that a divine reason was the ground of all phenomena. They employed for this the name λογος σπερματικος, because all being and life germinates from that logos as from a seed, and they also spoke in the plural of λογοι σπερματικοι, in order thereby to indicate alongside the unity also the diversity of the all-creating natural power, Zeller, Philos. d. Gr. IV³ 159, 160, cf. 140 noot. Even the later so significant distinction of λογος ενδιαθετος and προφορικος is borrowed from the Stoa, ib. 67 noot. This Greek doctrine of the idea, the νους and the λογος was now already before Philo connected with the doctrine of the O.T. concerning the word and the wisdom, Zeller, ib. V 266. But it was nevertheless especially Philo who fused all these various elements—Platonic doctrine of ideas, Stoic doctrine of the logos, Old Testament doctrine of wisdom, etc.—into one system. He proceeds from the dualism of God and world. God is αποιος, indescribable; we can only say that He is, not what He is. Therefore He also cannot stand in immediate contact with matter. Before God created the sensible world, He made a plan and formed in His thought the κοσμος νοητος, the ideas, as παραδειγματα and δυναμεις of all things. These ideas are now represented by Philo as the powers through which God can work in the world, and now more now less pictorially, now more now less personally described as servants, stewards, envoys, mediators, as λογοι and powers, as bands and pillars, which by Moses were called angels and by the Greeks daemons, as thoughts which are located in the divine thinking and are uncreated and infinite as God Himself. These ideas are now also many in number, but they find their unity in the logos, the idea that includes all ideas, the power that includes all powers, the book that contains all thoughts in itself, the κοσμος νοητος itself. And in the same manner as the divine ideas, so also is this logos described, now more as an attribute of God and one with His wisdom, and then more as a being distinguished from God. He stands, as it were, between God and world and has part in both; he is not uncreated as God and yet also not created as the finite things; he is deputy, envoy, interpreter, steward, angel, instrument, image, shadow of God, indeed His firstborn son, His eldest son in distinction from the world which is His youngest son; Philo even calls him God, but δευτερος θεος. Zeller places clearly in the light that the logos in Philo, just as the divine ideas, bears and must bear this double character. He is an intermediate being and therefore a double being, an attribute of God and yet a person, neither identical with God nor a creature as the world, an idea in the thinking of God and a power in the world, hovering back and forth between an impersonal attribute and a personal subsistence, but for that very reason deemed suitable as mediator between God and the world, cf. Zeller, Philos. d. Gr. V³ 370-386, and literature on Philo there 338, with also Max Heinze, Die Lehre v. Logos in der gr. Philos. 1872; Schürer, Neut. Zeitgesch. 653 f.; Meijer-Weiss, Ev. Joh. 6e Aufl. S. 52 f.; Jean Réville, La doctrine du Logos, Paris 1881; Kuenen, Volksgodsd. en wereldgodsd. 163 v.; Weiszäcker, Das apost. Zeitalter der chr. Kirche 1890 S. 549 f.; Hatch, Griechentum and Christentum 1892 S. 129 f.; Schultz, Altt. Theol.⁴ S. 552 f.; Kaftan, Das Verhältniss des evang. Glaubens zur Logoslehre (Gottschick, Zeits. f. Th. u. K. VII 1897 1es Heft S. 1-27); Anathon Aall, Geschichte der Logosidee in der griech. Philosophie. Leipzig Reisland 1896; Theol. Stud. v. Dr. Danbanton, XI 1893 2e aflev. In Jewish theology this doctrine of intermediate beings was developed even further. As completely transcendent, God cannot enter into immediate connection with creatures. He has need of all sorts of intermediate beings for this. If He wishes to do nothing other than guide the present powers in nature and humanity, then He makes use of angels. But if He wishes to work in the world creatively or re-creatively, hypostases appear, which, although they are creatures, nevertheless bear divine attributes, because they are representatives of God. Such hypostases are Metraton, the μεταθρονος of God; Memra, the word of God; Schechina, the presence of God’s glory; Bath Kol, the voice of God, which gives revelations and proclaims oracles; Ruach hakkodesch, the spirit which proceeds from God and communicates higher knowledge, Eisenmenger, Entdecktes Judenthum, I 265 f. II 393 f. etc.; Weber, System der altsyn. paläst. Theol. 172-189; Herzog² 2, 230 f., 13, 458 f.

3. Although the Wisdom of Jesus Sirach still attaches itself quite closely to the canonical literature, in the Book of Wisdom, with Philo, and in Jewish theology an influence of philosophy is to be perceived, which causes them to stray ever further from the thoughts of the O.T. First, there is a difference in principle. The doctrine of intermediate beings in Philo and later Jewish theology was born from the Platonic contrast of God and world, of which no trace is to be noticed in the O.T. books. The word and the wisdom in the O.T. are not mediating intermediate beings between God and world, but they stand entirely on the side of God, belong to Him, and are the principia of the created world. In Philo, however, the intermediate beings occupy an impossible place: they are neither God nor creature, neither person nor attribute, neither subsistence nor power, but have part in both; they erase the boundary line which in the O.T. always separates the creature from the Creator, and prepare the way for the philosophy of Gnosticism and the Kabbalah. In the second place, there is a no less great difference in the character which the doctrine of the word and wisdom in Scripture bears and which the doctrine of intermediate beings in Philo bears. In Philo the logos in its first meaning is identical with reason, thought, the concept in God, and thus as such immanent to God, nothing but an attribute. Only in its second meaning does that logos become word, which proceeds from God and occupies a mediating place between God and world. In the O.T., however, the word is not first reason and thought of God, much less an ideal world-image, a κοσμος νοητος, but the spoken word, through which He creates and sustains all things. And also the wisdom in Job and Proverbs is represented not as an attribute of God, but personally as possessed and ordered by God from eternity and consulted and examined at the creation of all things. In the third place, the intermediate beings in Philo and the Jewish theologians have no soteriological significance. They certainly serve for illumination and for communication of knowledge, but there is no mention of a connection between those intermediate beings and the Messiah. They even push the doctrine of the Messiah as revelation of the truth and as procurer of salvation into the background. And now it is indeed true that also in the O.T. the connection between word and wisdom, servant of God and Messiah, Malak Ihvh and Davidide, is not yet clearly executed. The lines still run alongside each other. But they nevertheless approach each other. Elohim and Jahveh is the same God. He who as Elohim creates and sustains the world through Word and Spirit, is also He who as Jahveh has led Israel through His Malak, will save His people through the servant of Jahveh, and will rule them eternally through the Messiah from the house of David, and will renew and sanctify them all through His Spirit. And these lines, which in the O.T. increasingly approach each other, run out to Him who is the Logos, the prophet, priest, and king, in whom God comes to His people and dwells eternally among them. For Philo an incarnation of the logos would have been an absurd thought. In the N.T. this is precisely the highest revelation of God. Finally, it may here be added that the doctrine of intermediate beings in Philo etc. is not finished and has no limit. Philo does bring some unity into the divine ideas by summarizing them in the logos, but the same thing that he says of this one, he also repeatedly says of all divine ideas. In Jewish theology the number of intermediate beings increases ever more. It is an emanation, as with the aeons in Gnosticism. The dualism takes its revenge to the very end. The intermediate beings establish no communion between God and world, because they are actually neither of the two. The world nevertheless always remains outside and over against God. The significance of the Spirit of God is not perceived. In the O.T. this doctrine of the Spirit occupies a great place. In the apocryphal literature, with Philo, and in Jewish theology it is almost entirely neglected. The Spirit is at most only a Spirit of prediction, granted to a few, Zeller, V 384, Weber, 184 f., but no longer the Spirit of the O.T., who concludes and completes creation and re-creation. For all these reasons there is a principial distinction between the development which the Trinitarian thoughts of the O.T. have found in the apocryphal literature, with Philo and the Jews, and that which is given to it in the N.T. The N.T. may have some words in common with Philo etc. and also speak of Christ as λογος, εικων, απαυγασμα, υἱος, θεος, etc.; the agreement goes no further. The N.T. is written in Greek, in the κοινη διαλεκτος, in the language that existed and was spoken everywhere. It created no new language. The thoughts of God have assumed the σαρξ of human language. But in those words God has placed a new content. There is agreement in form, the content differs. Philo and John have no more than the name of Logos in common.
