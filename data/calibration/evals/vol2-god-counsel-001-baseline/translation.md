§ 28. THE COUNSEL OF GOD.

1. Up to now we have treated of the Divine Being, as it is and exists in itself. Not in the sense, as if we thought and spoke about God apart from His revelation in nature and Scripture. For we cannot speak of God except on the ground of His revelation; if we venture to take His names upon our lips, we speak of Him as Christians, taught by His Word. But nevertheless we treated of God, as He exists in Himself according to His revelation. And we learned to know Him as the eternal Being, who at once is the highest being and the highest life, as summum esse, pure essentia and also as ipsissima ἐνεργεια, actus purissimus, totus actuosus; He always works. Now we descend to His works. Those works are the creatures, opera ad extra. But before we can consider these, the question arises: What is the connection between God, as He exists in Himself, and these His works? And that connection is indicated in the counsel of God, which includes the opera essentialia ad intra, i.e., the decrees. These are not described to us in Scripture in the abstract, but are set before our eyes in history itself. God is Lord of the whole earth and demonstrates that from day to day in the creation, maintenance, and government of all things. And so it is also with election and reprobation. These are not depicted in the O.T. as eternal decrees, but they meet us on every page as facts in history. From the beginning the human race divides into two, into the holy line of Seth, Gen. 4:25, 26, 5:1–32 and that of Cain, estranging himself more and more from God, Gen. 4:17–24. When both generations mix and cause unrighteousness to increase, Noah alone finds grace in the eyes of the Lord, Gen. 6. After the flood the blessing is pronounced over Shem and Japheth, but the curse upon Canaan, Gen. 9:25–27. From the generation of Shem Abraham is chosen, Gen. 12. Of his sons not Ishmael but Isaac is the son of the promise, Gen. 17:19–21, 21:12, 13. Among Isaac’s sons Esau is hated and Jacob loved, Gen. 25:23, Mal. 1:2, Rom. 9:11, 12. Of the sons of Jacob each receives his own rank and task, and Judah receives the primacy, Gen. 49. While all other nations are passed by temporarily and walk their own ways, Israel alone is chosen by the Lord as a people of possession. This election, ‎‏בחר‏‎, ‎‏ידע‏‎, Hos. 13:5, Am. 3:2, has not occurred on account of Israel’s worthiness but only according to God’s merciful love, Deut. 4:37, 7:6–8, 8:17, 9:4–6, 10:15, Ezek. 16:1f. Am. 9:7 and this love is from of old, Jer. 31:3. It had as its object Israel as people and nation, although thousands broke the covenant and there is a distinction between Israel according to the flesh and according to the promise, Rom. 2:28, 29, Rom. 9–11. And it had as its purpose that Israel would belong to the Lord, be His people and possession, and walk in holiness before His face, Ex. 19:5, Deut. 7:6, 14:2, 26:18, Ps. 135:4, Mal. 3:17.

Within the circle of Israel there is then repeatedly mention of a special election to an office or service, thus e.g., of Jerusalem and Zion to a dwelling of the Lord, Deut. 12:5, 14:23, 1 Kings 11:30, 2 Kings 21:7, Ps. 78:68, 70, of Moses as mediator of the O. Covenant, Ex. 3, of Levi to the priesthood, Deut. 18:5, 21:5, of Saul and David as king, 1 Sam. 10:24, 2 Sam. 6:21, of the prophets to their office, 1 Sam. 3, Isa. 6, Jer. 1, Ezek. 1–3, Am. 3:7, 8, 7:15, above all of the Messiah as Redeemer of His people, who is Israel, the servant of the Lord par excellence, Isa. 41:8, 42:1, 44:1, 45:4 etc. Yet although this election in the O.T. appears mostly as fact in history and thus coincides with the calling itself, nevertheless it rests upon a foreknowledge and foreordination of God. In general the O. Testament teaches that God creates, maintains, and governs all things by the word and with wisdom, Ps. 33:6, 104:24, Job 38, Prov. 8 etc., so that everything rests upon the thought of God. But it is also expressly said that God knows the future and proclaims it beforehand, Isa. 41:22, 23, 42:9, 43:9–12, 44:7, 46:10, 48:3f., Amos 3:7. In prophecy He makes known beforehand the things which and as they shall happen, Gen. 3:14f. 6:13, 9:25f. 12:2f. 15:13f. 25:23, 49:8f. etc. The days of a man’s life are all already determined beforehand and written in the book of God, before any have come into being, Ps. 139:16, 31:16, 39:6, Job 14:5. The righteous are written in the book of life, as the citizens of a city or a kingdom are enrolled, and have therein the assurance that they shall partake of life in the community with God in the theocracy of Israel, Ex. 32:32, Ps. 87:6, Ezek. 13:9, Jer. 17:13, Ps. 69:29; in Isa. 4:3 and Dan. 12:1 this is further developed so, that those who are written for life shall partake of the theocratic salvation in the future. The N.T. thought is here prepared for, that the book of life contains the names of the heirs of eternal life. And all things happen according to the counsel of God. With Him are wisdom and power, counsel, ‎‏עֵצָה‏‎ and understanding, Job 12:13, Prov. 8:14, Isa. 9:5, 11:2, 28:29, Jer. 32:19. He therefore always chooses the best ways to the attainment of His end, needs no one’s counsel, and is terrible, far exalted, above the counsel of the saints and of all who are around Him, Isa. 40:13, Jer. 23:18, 22, Ps. 89:8. God’s counsel is His definite thought, His firm decree concerning all things, Isa. 14:24–27, Dan. 4:24. That counsel is indeed hidden, Job 15:8 but comes to realization in history itself. For according to that counsel everything happens, it exists in eternity, no one can resist it, Isa. 24 vv. 24–27, 46:10, Ps. 33:11, Prov. 19:21; and in contrast the counsel of the enemies is destroyed, Neh. 4:15, Ps. 33:10, Prov. 21:30, Jer. 19:7.

2. The N. Test. sets this counsel of God in a much clearer light. Not only are all His works known to God from eternity, Acts 15:18 (cf. however the variae lectiones), but everything happens according to the determined counsel and foreknowledge of God. The N.T. word βουλη indicates the will of God as resting upon counsel and deliberation, and is thereby distinguished from θελημα, the will as will, cf. Eph. 1:11 βουλη του θεληματος. Such a counsel of God precedes all things. It includes everything, Eph. 1:11, also the sinful deeds of men, Acts 2:23, 4:28, cf. Luke 22:22. God has predetermined the dwelling place of the nations both in time and in extent, ὁρισας, Acts 17:26. God’s will is manifest also in the perdition of Judas, John 17:12, in the giving over of the Gentiles, Rom. 1:24, in the rejection of Esau, Rom. 9:13, in the hardening of the ungodly, Rom. 9:18, in the raising up of Pharaoh, Rom. 9:17, in the enduring of the vessels of wrath fitted for destruction, Rom. 9:22, in the being set (κειμαι) of Christ not only for a resurrection but also εἰς πτωσιν, Luke 2:34, for a κρισις, John 3:19–21, for a λιθος προσκομματος και πετρα σκανδαλου, 1 Pet. 2:7, 8, cf. 1 Thess. 5:9, Jude v. 4. But especially the βουλη του θεου has reference to the work of redemption, Luke 7:30, Acts 13:36, 20:27, Heb. 6:17.

And the N.T. is rich in words to describe this counsel of God more closely. It speaks of εὐδοκια, good pleasure, delight, Mt. 11:26, Luke 2:14, 10:21, Eph. 1:5, 9, Phil. 2:13, 2 Thess. 1:11; προθεσις, purpose, Rom. 8 v. 28, 9:11, Eph. 1:11, 3:11, 2 Tim. 1:9; προγνωσις, foreknowledge, Rom. 8:29, 11:2, 1 Pet. 1:2; ἐκλογη, election, Mark 13:20, Acts 9:15, 13:17, 15:7, Rom. 9:11, 11:5, 28, 1 Cor. 1:27, 28, Eph. 1:4, 1 Thess. 1:4, 2 Pet. 1:10, James 2:5; προορισμος, Rom. 8:29, 2 Cor. 2:7, Eph. 1:5, 11; cf. also Acts 13 v. 48 where it is said that as many as believed were ὁσοι ἠσαν τεταγμενοι εἰς ζωην αἰωνιον, i.e., not as if they had prepared themselves or were subjectively predisposed but as those who were ordained, appointed to eternal life; and also Eph. 2:10, where of the good works of believers it is said that God προητοιμασεν them.

The words are thus distinguished: προθεσις indicates that God in the work of salvation proceeds not according to arbitrary will or chance but according to a fixed plan, according to an immutable purpose; ἐκλογη modifies this thus, that that purpose does not include all, but that it is a προθεσις κατ’ ἐκλογην, Rom. 9:11, an electing purpose, so that not all but many come to salvation; προγνωσις regards the persons who in this electing purpose of God are the object not of His nuda praescientia but of His dilectio practica; while finally προορισμος looks more to the means which God ordains to bring these known ones to the destination appointed for them. Προθεσις certitudinem eventus indicat, προγνωσις personarum singularitatem, προορισμος mediorum ordinem. Election is certa per προθεσιν, determinata per προγνωσιν, ordinata per προορισμον, Turretinus, Theol. El. IV qu. 7.

The eternity of this purpose is not of itself already enclosed in the preposition προ with which the words are composed, but is nevertheless clearly expressed in Eph. 3:11, 2 Tim. 1:9, cf. Mt. 25:34, 1 Cor. 2:7, Eph. 1:4. It has indeed been asserted that Paul in Rom. 9 does not treat of the absolute freedom and the eternal decree of God, but only of a Verhalten Gottes, das in der Zeit, in der Geschichte seine Ursachen wie seine Wirkungen hat, W. Beyschlag, Die paulin. Theodicee Röm. IX–XI, 2te Aufl. Halle 1895. Dr. van Dĳk, De leer der verkiezing volgens het N. T., Studiën ed. by de la Saussaye, Valeton and Van Dĳk IV 3rd part. Buhl, Der Gedankengang von Röm. 9–11, Stud. u. Kr. 1887 S. 295–322. Kübel, Herzog² 12, 158. But this assertion is refuted by Rom. 9. For, the προθεσις κατ’ ἐκλογην clearly precedes the facts of history; history thus serves to make that purpose, which already existed, remain firm, Rom. 9:11. To Sarah a seed of promise was promised long beforehand, v. 9. Before the children of Isaac were born, God had already said that the greater would serve the lesser, v. 11. Verses 15–18 teach that election has its causes not in works but solely and alone in the will of the one who calls. To be sure, Rom. 9 speaks of an acting of God in time but the cause of that acting falls outside of time, lies alone in God’s will and good pleasure. Besides, the causa electionis elsewhere is also clearly attributed alone and exclusively to God’s grace, love, good pleasure, Mt. 11:25, Luke 12:32, Eph. 1:5, 9, 11, 2 Tim. 1:9, 10. Indeed it is not correct, with regard to Rom. 9:21, to think exclusively either with supralapsarianism of the massa incorrupta or with Augustine and infralapsarianism only of the massa corrupta. Paul here does not think at all of this distinction. What he wishes to say is only this, that God has the absolute right to give to His creatures that destiny which seems good to Him, cf. Isa. 10:15, Jer. 18, Mt. 20:15. From the standpoint of absolute right a creature has nothing to say against his Creator. Paul refutes all objections against election not by [arguing] that it is righteous and equitable but with an appeal to the sovereignty and absolute right of God.

Just as the N.T. expresses the eternity of election more clearly than the O.T., so it also conceives of that election more individually and personally. While in the O.T. the people of Israel appears as the object of election, here they are particular persons who are chosen in Christ, together form His body, and bear the name of εκλεκτοι, Mt. 24:31, Luke 18:7, Acts 13:48, Rom. 8:33, Eph. 1:4, Tit. 1:1, 2 Tim. 2:10, 1 Pet. 1 v. 1, 2, 9 etc. This appears especially from the book of life, in which are written the names of the heirs of eternal life, Luke 10:20, Heb. 12:23, Phil. 4:3, Rev. 3:5, 13:8, 20:12, 21:27, 22:19.

Finally it is the clear teaching of the N. Test. that election has its purpose, not in an earthly life in Canaan, nor also in an eminent place in the Kingdom of God, but specifically in heavenly blessedness. To be sure, within the congregation there is also again an election to one or another office or service, e.g., of the apostles, Luke 6:13, John 6 v. 70 etc., but the proper election has as its purpose holiness, Eph. 1:4, sonship, Eph. 1:5, blessedness, 2 Thess. 2 v. 13, eternal life, Acts 13:48, conformity to Christ, Rom. 8:29, John 17:24, the glorification of God, Eph. 1 vv. 6, 12. Also in Rom. 9 Paul does not treat of a higher or lower place in the Kingdom of God, but he makes a distinction in Israel itself between children of the flesh and children of the promise. He speaks specifically of vessels for honor and of vessels for dishonor, κατηρτισμενα εἰς ἀπωλειαν. He places mercy and hardening in opposition to each other, and lets in vv. 14 and 19 objections come to word, whose seriousness precisely presupposes the doctrine of such a free election.

3. Also outside of the Christian religion various conflicts have been waged concerning predestination and freedom of the will. Philosophy has alternately arrived at a pantheistic determinism or at a deistic doctrine of freedom. The Jews ascribe to man also in the state of sin freedom of the will, Weber, System 223. In Mohammedanism a conflict has been waged concerning predestination and freedom of the will, which in many respects is analogous to that in the Christian church. In Islam God is the absolute omnipotence, the absolute arbitrary will, over against which man is completely passive. Against this there arose in the second century of the Hedjra resistance from the side of the Mutazilites, who defended free will, contested predestination, and regarded not omnipotence but righteousness as the essence of God, Houtsma, De strijd over het dogma tot op El-Ash’ari, Leiden 1875. Kuenen, Volksgodsd. en wereldgodsd. 1882 p. 40 f.; cf. also the conflict in the school of Ramanuja in India, which is often compared with that of the Gomarists and Arminians, Saussaye, Rel. gesch. I 448.

In the Christian church in the first period one placed all emphasis, over against the heathen fate and the gnostic naturalism, upon the moral nature, freedom, and responsibility of man and could therefore not let the teaching of Scripture concerning the counsel of God come to its right. Man was indeed corrupted by sin in greater or lesser measure but nevertheless remained free and could accept the offered grace of God. An absolute predestination and an irresistible grace was not taught; the counsel of God consisted in the praescientia and the dependent determination of reward or punishment. God gives over to unbelief those of whom He foreknows that they will not believe, and He chooses those whose merits He has foreseen, Justin, c. Tryph. § 141. Iren. adv. haer. IV 29. Tertull. adv. Marc. II 23 etc., cf. Münscher–v. Coelln I 356 f. Hagenbach, § 48 and 57. Calvin, Inst. II 2, 4, 9. This has essentially remained the standpoint of the Greek church.

Man has become weakened and mortal through sin. Nevertheless he can still will the naturally good and also accept or reject the offered grace (gratia praeveniens). If he accepts it, he is supported by that grace (gratia cooperans), and must persevere to the end, for he can still always fall away. Those who accept the grace and persevere, God has foreknown and destined for blessedness. The others, although He voluntate antecedente wills the salvation of all, He leaves lying in their fall and destines them for perdition, Damascene, de fide orthod. II 29, 30. Conf. orthod. qu. 26–30.

In the manner of expression Pelagius joined the ancients, but he nevertheless taught, by failing to recognize the doctrine of redemption which the church fathers always maintained at the same time, something essentially new, Harnack, D. G. III 183. Sin with him was not a habit or state, but only an act, which leaves human nature with its free will completely uninjured. Grace consists therein, that God gives to man through creation the posse in natura and furthermore in the law and the doctrine and the example of Christ offers him an auxilium divinum; this gratia per Christum is however granted secundum merita to such as use their free will well. Predestination finally was nothing but a foreseeing by God of the free deeds and merits of men and the corresponding foreordination of reward and punishment. Properly speaking there is thus no predestination on the part of God, neither to grace nor to blessedness; it depends entirely upon the praevisio of the good deeds of man. Only Pelagius came into great difficulty with infant baptism, for this was granted without any merits; and he could not extricate himself from this except through all kinds of subterfuges and inconsistencies.

The teaching of Pelagius was somewhat softened in the so-called semi-pelagianism of John Cassian, abbot in Marseille, pupil of Chrysostom and well acquainted with the Greek fathers. He did teach that sin had corrupted human nature. But man was not dead, but sick. He was like a sick person, who cannot heal himself but can nevertheless take the medicine to himself and long for healing; to someone who has fallen into a pit, [who] cannot save himself but can nevertheless grasp the rope thrown to him. The sinful man can therefore not merit grace, but can nevertheless accept it, and, supported by it, persevere. And God grants that grace to those of whom He has foreseen that they would accept it and persevere in it; thus also with children and peoples. He withholds it on the contrary from those of whom He foresees the opposite. And herein consists predestination and reprobation; it depends upon the praescientia Dei concerning the attitude of man toward the offered grace. Nostrum est velle, Dei perficere. Cf. G. J. Vossius, Historia Pelagianismi 1618, better edition 1655. Wiggers, Pragm. Darstellung des Aug. u. Pelag. 1831–33. Wörter, Der Pelag. nach seinem Ursprunge und seiner Lehre, Freiburg 1866. Munscher-v. Coelln I 371 f. Harnack, D. G. III 151 f. Schwane, Dogm. II² 512 f. Möller, art. Pelagius in Herzog².

Already long before the Pelagian conflict Augustine taught predestination. He came to this through his study of the letter to the Romans, Reuter, Augustin. Studien 1887 S. 5 f., and wished therein to give nothing other than the teaching of Scripture, de dono pers. c. 19. He gave it already in his quaestiones ad Simplicianum of the year 397 and developed it then more broadly in his writings de correptione et gratia 427, de praedestinatione sanctorum and de dono perseverantiae 428 or 429. Augustine makes a distinction between praescientia and praedestinatio. The former is broader than the latter. Praedestinasse est hoc praescisse, quod (Deus) fuerat ipse facturus, de dono persev. 18. de praed. sanct. 10. 19. The praedestinatio is nothing other than praescientia scilicet et praeparatio beneficiorum Dei, quibus certissime liberantur quicunque liberantur, de dono pers. 14. This predestination happens not according to merit or worth but out of pure grace; not in order to but unto faith, non eliguntur quia crediderunt sed eliguntur ut credant, de praed. sanct. 17. All men were after all equal, a massa damnata, de civ. XIV 26. de praed. sanct. 8. de nat. et gr. 4. 8 etc. This appears especially in the predestination of young children, of whom some perish without baptism and others are saved through baptism, de praed. sanct. 12. Enchir. 98.

Predestination has its sole cause in the sovereign will of God, in His absolute freedom. He owes no one anything, and can justly condemn all, but according to His good pleasure He makes one vessel for honor and the other for dishonor, de praed. sanct. 8. Alongside predestination thus stands reprobation. Augustine reckons this often to the praedestinatio. He speaks of a praedestinare ad aeternam mortem, de an. et ejus orig. IV 10. de civ. XXII 24, of praedestinati ad sempiternum interitum, in Joh. Ev. tract. 48, of mundus damnationi praedestinatus, ib. 111, of Judas as perditioni praedestinatus, ib. 107 etc. The text 1 Tim. 2:4 was therefore explained by Augustine in a limited sense and in various ways, Enchir. 103. de corr. et gr. 14. Mostly however praedestinatio is understood as predestination to blessedness. With respect to the good praedestinatio is necessary, with respect to evil praescientia is sufficient, although this is nevertheless again conceived not merely passively, but actively. For God does not ordain in the same manner unto destruction and unto the means leading thereto, namely sins, as He ordains unto blessedness and unto the means which serve thereto. The praedestinatio is namely with Augustine always a praedestinatio adaequata, i.e., unto blessedness and therefore also unto grace. To the praedestinati belong also those who do not yet believe or are not even yet born. But their number stands fast and is unchangeable. In time they come to Christ, receive baptism, faith, and above all also the donum perseverantiae. For this is granted only to the praedestinati. Whether someone is predestined, he can only know from this, that he perseveres to the end. For God has received into the church some who are not chosen and do not persevere, in order that the praedestinati might not exalt themselves and seek false rest, de corr. et gr. 13. Why God now saves only some and lets others perish is a mystery. It is not unjust, for He owes no one anything. Reprobation is an act of righteousness, just as praedestinatio was an act of grace. In both God makes His virtues manifest, de civ. XIV 26, cf. Wiggers, I 290 f. Schwane II² 557 f. Baltzer, Des h. Aug. Lehre über Prädest. und Reprob.
