§ 21. THE KNOWABILITY OF GOD. (Cognitio Dei insita.)

1. As previously with regard to religion I 209 ff., so also now with regard to the knowledge of God, it appeared that it cannot have its origin except in revelation. If God does not reveal Himself in His creatures, then of itself no knowledge of Him is possible either. But if He displays His virtues in the world of creatures, then the knowability of God can no longer be contested either. Naturally, therewith the nature and the measure of that knowledge is not determined. All who teach the knowability of God willingly agree that this knowledge is entirely sui generis and of very limited scope. For even if God is revealed to some extent in His creatures, there remains in Him an infinite fullness of power and life behind, which does not come to light. His knowledge and power does not coincide with the world and does not pour itself out into it fully. He cannot even perfectly reveal Himself to and in creatures, for the finite does not comprehend the infinite, no one knows the Father except the Son, Matt. 11:27 cf. Deut. 29:29. Moreover, that which God reveals of Himself in and through creatures is already so rich and so deep that it can never be fully known by any human. We do not understand in so many respects the world of created things and stand at every moment on all sides before riddles and mysteries; how then should we understand the revelation of God in all its richness and depth? But with that the knowability of God is nevertheless not nullified. The incomprehensibility of God nullifies this knowability so little that it rather presupposes and confirms it; the richness of the Divine being, surpassing all knowledge, constitutes precisely a necessary and important element of our knowledge of God. It remains established that God is knowable to us in the same manner and to the same extent as He reveals Himself to us in creatures.

That there is now a revelation of God in the world can actually hardly be denied. First, Holy Scripture leaves us in no doubt regarding this. It erects no altar to the unknown God, but proclaims that God who made the world, Acts 17:23, whose power and divinity can be beheld from the creatures through the human νοῦς, Rom. 1:19, who above all created man in His image and likeness, Gen. 1:26, as His offspring, who lives and moves in Him, Acts 17:28, has spoken to him through prophets and apostles, above all through His Son Himself, Heb. 1:1, and now continually reveals Himself to and in him through Word and Spirit, Matt. 16:17, John 14:23, etc. According to Holy Scripture the whole world is a creation and thus also a revelation of God; there is in an absolute sense nothing atheistic. And this testimony of Scripture is confirmed from all sides. There is no atheistic world, there are no atheistic peoples, there are also no atheistic human beings. The world cannot be thought as ἄθεος, because it then could not be the work of God but would have to be the creation of an ἀντίθεος. And now, the dualism that has repeatedly appeared in religion and philosophy has indeed regarded a daemonic principle in matter, but in the world has always recognized a connection of idea and matter, a struggle of light and darkness. There is no one who can deny in an absolute sense and consistently the knowability of God, and thus His revelation in the world entirely. Agnosticism itself is proof of this, just as skepticism cannot maintain itself except with the help of what it combats. And precisely therefore, because the world is not to be thought ἄθεος, therefore there are also no atheistic and irreligious peoples. Indeed this has been asserted by Socinus, Tract. Theol. c. 2, de auctor. Script. c. 2, Locke, Essay on human understanding I ch. 4 § 8, and by many in this century, e.g. Büchner, Kraft u. Stoff¹⁶ 380 f., Darwin, Afst. des menschen 1884 I 127. But this sentiment has been sufficiently refuted and now almost generally conceded, Moor, Comm. I 57. M. Vitringa, Comm. I 16. Flint, Antitheistic theories, 3 ed. p. 250-289, 519-532. Peschel, Völkerkunde S 260. Zöckler, Das Kreuz Christi 1875 S. 417 f. The well-known statement of Cicero, that there is no people so barbarous that it does not believe in the gods, has been confirmed from age to age. This fact is of great significance. That in which all human beings agree by virtue of their nature cannot be false, Cic. de nat. deor. I 17. Opinionum enim commenta delet dies, naturae judicia confirmat, ib. II 2. And so there are finally also no atheistic human beings. There is not so much difference regarding the existence as rather regarding the being of God. Indeed there is a practical atheism, a life without God in the world, Ps. 14:1, 53:2, Eph. 2:12. But conscious theoretical atheism in an absolute sense is rare, if it ever occurs. The word atheism, however, is often employed in a relative sense, not as denial of the Deity in general, but of a particular Deity. The Greeks accused Socrates of atheism, Xenophon, Memor. I 1. Cicero counted Protagoras, Prodicus among the atheists, because they denied the popular gods, de nat. deor. I 42. The Christians were therefore often accused of atheism by the Heathens, Suicerus s. v. ἄθεος. And the Christians in turn employed that name for those who denied the God of revelation, ib. The Romans have sometimes counted Luther, Melanchthon, Calvin among the atheists, Buddeus, de Atheismo 1747 p. 116 sq. In more recent times J. G. Fichte was openly accused of atheism, because he held the moral world-order itself to be God. And still the name of atheist is given to those who know no other power than matter, such as Feuerbach, Strauss, Büchner, Haeckel, Czolbe, Dühring, cf. A. Drews, Die deutsche Spekulation seit Kant II 235 f. And indeed, if the materialists recognize nothing other than matter and the changing of matter, then they are atheists and wish themselves to be held as such. But this almost never occurs. Taken in an absolute sense, as denial of an absolute power, then atheism is almost unthinkable. All finally recognize again a power that is worshipped as God. Strauss asks for his universe a piety, as the believer for his God, Der alte u. d. neue Gl². 141 f. Atheism and materialism repeatedly turn into pantheism, Büchner, Kraft u. Stoff¹⁶ 3, 70, 71 f., Haeckel, Nat. Schöpfungsgesch. 20, 32, 64 Id. Der Monismus als Band zw. Rel. u. Wiss. 1893. This occurs because man cannot escape the recognition of a highest power. At the same moment that he denies the true God, he adorns himself with a false God. Religion sits too deep in human nature for that, and the revelation of God speaks too loudly. Even when in some times religious indifferentism and skepticism grasp far and wide, such as e.g. in the age of Pericles, of Emperor Augustus, of the Renaissance, and also in this century, nevertheless religion always comes back up. Rather man seizes upon the grossest superstition than that he can endure in the end with the naked, cold unbelief. But we can go even further. Not only does atheism in an absolute sense almost never occur, but even in the sense that it would deny a personal God who has claim to our worship, it is rare. Naturalism, hylozoism, pantheism are undoubtedly directions that repeatedly present themselves. But they are directions not so much in the religious as rather in the philosophical domain. They have never arisen of themselves, spontaneously, but owe their origin to criticism of the religious representations in others. They are not dogmatic but critical; and therefore they always serve only for a time and in a limited circle. A people, a society, a church, a congregation of such naturalists and pantheists is unthinkable and incapable of existence; the pantheists themselves recognize this, the religious representation is necessary for the common man, but only the philosopher elevates himself to the pure concept. From this follows that the belief in a personal God is natural and normal; it arises everywhere spontaneously and in all human beings. But atheism, even as denial of a personal God, is exception. It is philosophy, not religion. There is truth in the sharp word of Schopenhauer: ein unpersönlicher Gott ist gar kein Gott, sondern bloss ein missbrauchtes Wort, ein Unbegriff, eine contradictio in adjecto, ein Shiboleth für Philosophieprofessoren, welche, nachdem sie die Sache haben aufgeben müssen, mit dem Worte durchzuschleichen bemüht sind, Par. u. Parel. I⁵ 123. cf. Welt als Wille u. Vorst. II⁶ 398, 406, 739. Therefore there is also a certain will necessary in order not to believe in a God, in a personal God. Deum non esse non credit, nisi cui Deum non esse expedit. There are no athei who are firmly and certainly convinced of their unbelief, even to the point of martyrdom. Because it is abnormal and unnatural, resting not on immediate apprehensions but on mediate proofs and fallible reasonings, therefore it is never certain of its case. The proofs for the existence of a personal God may be weak, they are always still stronger than those for the denial. To prove that there is no God is even impossible; for that one would have to be omniscient and omnipresent, that is, God Himself, cf. Voetius, de atheismo, Disp. I 114-225. G. J. Vossius, de origine et progressu idol. I c. 3. Leydecker, Fax Veritatis III controv. 3. Turretinus, Theol. El. III qu. 2. Maresius, Syst. Theol. 1673 p. 44. Buddeus, Theses theol. de atheismo et superst., ed. Lulofs L. B. 1747. Hodge, Syst. Theol. I 198, 242. Flint, Antith. theories³, lecture I, Hoekstra, The Christian's Piety p. 6. Doedes, Introduction to the Doctrine of God, 57 ff.


2. This naturalness, universality, and necessity of religion and knowledge of God has already early led to the thought that these were innate and inborn in man by nature. The fact lies there indeed, that all human beings from earliest youth have consciousness not only of a physical but also of a psychical, spiritual, invisible world. True and false, good and evil, right and wrong, beautiful and ugly are indeed no weighable and measurable quantities and cannot be perceived with the five senses, but they are a reality that stands for our consciousness much more firmly than that of matter and of force. Materialism may reckon only with gravity and heat and electricity; but faith and hope and love are yet entirely different forces that have governed humanity and preserved it from sinking into bestiality. Augustinus has rightly said that the truth of spiritual things is actually much more certain and firm than that of visible things. Nihil absurdius dici potest, quam ea esse quae oculis videmus, ea vero non esse quae intelligentia cernimus, cum dubitare dementis sit intelligentiam incomparabiliter oculis anteferri, de immort. an. c. 10 n. 17. de civ. XIX c. 18. The truths of mathesis, of logic, the fundamental principles of ethics, law, religion stand indubitably firm for all. They bear a character of naturalness, universality, and necessity that cannot be denied by anyone. They appear as ideae innatae enclosed in human nature, given with his birth.

The doctrine of innate concepts is already rooted in Greek philosophy. There it made a great problem for thought how learning is possible at all. For one of two things seemed to be the case: either we already know something and then we cannot learn it anymore, or we do not know it, but how then is it to be explained that we strive after it in order to learn it, Zeller, Philos. d. Gr. I⁴ 996, II⁴ 823, III 189. Plato solved this problem through his doctrine of recollection; the soul had beheld the ideas in all their beauty before its connection with the body and preserved the images thereof deep in its memory. He proved this especially with mathematics, which we can construct entirely from our mind without the help of sense perception, but otherwise thought that all learning presupposed the pre-existence of the soul, Zeller, ib. II⁴ 639, 643 f. 823 f. Aristoteles indeed regarded sense perception as the way to knowledge, but judged nevertheless that reason brought along potentially some highest, general principles that stand firm through themselves, lie at the foundation of all proofs, and are recognized by all, ib. III 188 f. The Stoics spoke of κοιναὶ ἔννοιαι, φυσικαὶ ἔννοιαι, ἔμφυτοι προλήψεις, that is, such concepts which by virtue of the nature of our thinking are derived by all from perception, ib. IV³ 74 f. 389 f. With all these philosophers there is as yet no question of innate ideas in the strict sense. Plato limits recollection not to some innate concepts but extends it to all knowledge; and Aristoteles and others indeed speak of general principles but teach expressly that these are not given as ideas with birth but are found specifically through the means of perception and thought, but in such a way that every normal human being must then find them. In the proper sense the doctrine of innate concepts first appears with Cicero. He speaks of notiones impressae, cogitationes insitae, innatae and assumes a knowledge of all kinds of truths prior to all experience and investigation. There are according to him semina innata virtutum, Tusc. III 1, 2, notitiae parvae rerum maximarum, which nature sine doctrina planted in our soul, de fin. V 21, 59, an innate knowledge of God, Deos esse natura opinamur, Tusc. I 16, 36. Nat. D. I 1, 2 etc. Zeller, IV³ 659 f.


3. In modern philosophy the doctrine of ideae innatae was advanced by Cartesius, who also first used this term and thus employed idea in a sense unusual until that time. With Cartesius this doctrine was connected with his dualism between soul and body. The cognitio intellectualis is not to be derived from sense perception; this gives only the occasio, by which our mind per innatam facultatem forms the representations and concepts, Renati Descartes, Notae p. 185. The cognitio proceeds from a proper principle, that is, from ideae innatae. Among those ideae innatae that of God is the principal, which is as it were a nota artificis, operi suo impressa. Medit. tertia p. 24. But he conceives the being innate of those ideas in such a way that the soul possesses by nature the power, the facultas, the dispositio to produce them from itself. The ideas are thus not actual but potential in our mind, Object. et Respons. p. 102, Notae p. 184, 185. Also according to Leibniz the necessary and universal truths do not come to us from without, but they proceed from ourselves, such as e.g. those of substance, duration, change, cause, the mathematical truths and especially also the idea of God. Together these truths form the natural light of reason. But the being innate of those truths is explained by Leibniz more extensively and clearly than by Cartesius. The latter said only that those ideas were potential in our mind. Leibniz, however, says that they are innate virtualiter, comme des inclinations, des dispositions, des habitudes ou des virtualités naturelles et non pas comme des actions. The mind of man has not merely the receptivity to know them, for then all knowledge could be called innate, but it is the source of those truths, it can bring them forth from itself, les tirer lui-même de son fond. The ideas thus lie as it were preformed in the human soul. And this is possible because there is a representing and thinking without consciousness. They come to consciousness when sense perception gives occasion to it. This brings the ideas virtually present in us also actually into our mind, cf. Spruijt, Proeve van de leer der aangeb. begrippen 144, Stöckl, Gesch. d. n. Phil. I 426, Malebranche thought that learning and knowing in man could be explained only through the supposition that we see the ideas in God and that God thus as the universal and infinite being is immediately present in our understanding. Following in his footsteps, the ontologism of Gioberti, Gratry, Ubaghs, etc. taught that we immediately behold God in our mind as the absolute being and that thus an intuitive knowledge of God is present in man, Stöckl, Gesch. d. neuern Philos. II 568 f. 620 f. By Kant this doctrine of innate concepts was importantly modified. In connection with the terminology of Wolff he spoke of knowledge apriori and aposteriori, and now taught no innate concepts but rather innate forms, namely forms of intuition, that is, space and time, forms of understanding, that is, categories, and forms of reason, that is, the ideas God, virtue and immortality. The idealism of Fichte and Hegel pushed this doctrine to such a point that they constructed not only the knowledge of necessary and universal truths, but all knowledge, and even all being, the entire material world, from thinking. The grounds on which this doctrine of ideae innatae mainly rests are these: Learning, the capacity to learn, presupposes that what is learned is already present in some manner in our mind. Reasoning and proof are built on principles that must stand firm per se and apriori. Experience gives only δόξα, contingent truths; universal and necessary truths can proceed only from the mind of man itself. That there are such universal and necessary truths is proved by the consensus gentium. And above all, the opposition between soul and body is of such a nature that representations and concepts cannot have their origin in sense perception; they must be explained either from the mind of man or from the Spirit of God, in whom man beholds all ideas. On the other hand, the doctrine of innate concepts was contested by the Socinians, who rejected natural religion, Fock, Der Socin. 307 f., cf. also Episcopius, Inst. theol. I c. 3, Locke, Essay concerning human understanding I ch. 2-4, Hobbes, de cive c. 14 n. 19 etc. They appealed to the following considerations: The doctrine of ideae innatae is completely superfluous, because the origin of those ideas also allows itself to be well explained in another way. History teaches that no single representation and no single concept is the same among all human beings and peoples. Even innate moral principles do not exist; over good and evil the greatest possible difference prevails; and the concept of God is so little innate that even atheistic human beings and peoples exist. Children, idiots, the insane know therefore nothing of such innate concepts. All knowledge in man stems from sense perception; nihil est in intellectu quod non prius fuerit in sensu. This attack on the ideae innatae made much progress in England and France in the last century. The materialism of this century joined itself thereto. The doctrine of innate concepts has been generally abandoned and replaced by the theory of the inheritance of properties, Darwin, Afst. v. d. mensch, ch. 3, 4. Spencer, in Spruyt Proeve 342. Büchner, Kraft u. Stoff¹⁶ S. 344 f.
