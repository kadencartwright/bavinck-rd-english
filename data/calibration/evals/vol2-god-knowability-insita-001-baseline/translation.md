§ 21. THE KNOWABILITY OF GOD. (Cognitio Dei insita.)


1. Just as previously regarding religion I 209 ff., so it now appears likewise regarding the knowledge of God, that it cannot have its origin except in revelation. If God is not revealed in His creatures, then no knowledge of Him is possible of itself. But when He displays His virtues in the world of creatures, then the knowability of God can no longer be disputed. Naturally, therewith the nature and measure of that knowledge is not determined. All who teach the knowability of God readily agree that this knowledge is entirely sui generis and of very limited scope. For although God is revealed to some degree in His creatures, there remains in Him an infinite fullness of power and life behind, which does not come to light. His knowledge and power do not coincide with the world and do not pour themselves out fully into it. He cannot even reveal Himself perfectly to and in creatures, for the finite cannot contain the infinite; no one knows the Father except the Son, Mt. 11:27 cf. Deut. 29:29. Moreover, that which God reveals of Himself in and through creatures is already so rich and so deep that it can never be fully known by any human being. We do not understand in so many respects the world of created things and stand at every moment on all sides before riddles and mysteries; how then should we understand the revelation of God in all its richness and depth? But with this the knowability of God is nevertheless not nullified. The incomprehensibility of God nullifies this knowability so little that it rather presupposes and confirms it; the richness of the Divine being, surpassing all knowledge, constitutes precisely a necessary and important element of our knowledge of God. It remains fixed that God is knowable to us in the same manner and to the same degree as He reveals Himself to us in creatures.

That there is now a revelation of God in the world can actually hardly be denied. First, Holy Scripture leaves us in no doubt regarding this. It erects no altar to the unknown God, but proclaims that God who made the world, Hd. 17:23, whose power and divinity can be beheld from the creatures through the human νους, Rom. 1:19, who above all created man in His own image and likeness, Gen. 1:26, as His offspring, who lives and moves in Him, Hd. 17:28, who has spoken to him through prophets and apostles, above all through His own Son, Heb. 1:1, and now continually reveals Himself to and in him through Word and Spirit, Mt. 16:17, Joh. 14:23 etc. According to Holy Scripture the whole world is a creation and thus also a revelation of God; in the absolute sense there is nothing atheistic. And this testimony of Scripture is confirmed on all sides. There is no atheistic world, there are no atheistic peoples, there are also no atheistic human beings. The world cannot be thought as ἀθεος, for then it could not be the work of God but would have to be the creation of an ἀντιθεος. And now dualism, which has repeatedly appeared in religion and philosophy, has indeed regarded matter as a daemonic principle, but in the world has always acknowledged a connection of idea and matter, a struggle of light and darkness. There is no one who can in the absolute sense and consistently deny the knowability of God, and thus His revelation in the world entirely and completely. Agnosticism itself is proof of this, just as skepticism cannot maintain itself except with the help of what it combats. And precisely therefore, because the world cannot be thought as ἀθεος, therefore there are also no atheistic and religionless peoples. To be sure, this has been asserted by Socinus, Tract. Theol. c. 2, de auctor. Script. c. 2, Locke, Essay on human understanding I ch. 4 § 8, and by many in this century, e.g. Büchner, Kraft u. Stoff¹⁶ 380 f., Darwin, Afst. des menschen 1884 I 127. But this opinion has been sufficiently refuted and now almost universally acknowledged, Moor, Comm. I 57. M. Vitringa, Comm. I 16. Flint, Antitheïstic theories, 3 ed. p. 250-289, 519-532. Peschel, Völkerkunde S 260. Zöckler, Das Kreuz Christi 1875 S. 417 f. The well-known pronouncement of Cicero, that there is no people so barbarous that it does not believe in gods, has been confirmed from age to age. This fact is of great significance. That in which all human beings agree by virtue of their nature cannot be false, Cic. de nat. deor. I 17. Opinionum enim commenta delet dies, naturae judicia confirmat, ib. II 2. And thus there are finally also no atheistic human beings. There is not so much difference regarding the existence as regarding the being of God. To be sure, there is a practical atheism, a life without God in the world, Ps. 14:1, 53:2, Ef. 2:12. But conscious theoretical atheism in the absolute sense is rare, if it ever occurs. However, the word atheism is frequently used in a relative sense, not as denial of the Deity generally, but of a particular Deity. The Greeks accused Socrates of atheism, Xenophon, Memor. I 1. Cicero reckoned Protagoras, Prodicus among the atheists, because they denied the popular gods, de nat. deor. I 42. Christians were therefore often accused of atheism by the heathens, Suicerus s. v. ἀθεος. And Christians in turn used that name for those who denied the God of revelation, ib. The Romans have sometimes reckoned Luther, Melanchthon, Calvin among the atheists, Buddeus, de Atheismo 1747 p. 116 sq. In more recent times J. G. Fichte was openly accused of atheism, because he held the moral world-order itself to be God. And still the name of atheist is given to those who know no other power than matter, such as Feuerbach, Strauss, Büchner, Haeckel, Czolbe, Dühring, cf. A. Drews, Die deutsche Spekulation seit Kant II 235 f. And indeed, if the materialists recognize nothing other than matter and the changing of matter, then they are atheists and wish to be regarded as such. But this almost never occurs. Taken in the absolute sense, as denial of an absolute power, atheism is almost unthinkable. All finally acknowledge again a power that is worshipped as God. Strauss demands for his universe a piety, such as the believer has for his God, Der alte u. d. neue Gl². 141 f. Atheism and materialism repeatedly turn into pantheism, Büchner, Kraft u. Stoff¹⁶ 3, 70, 71 f., Haeckel, Nat. Schöpfungsgesch. 20, 32, 64 Id. Der Monismus als Band zw. Rel. u. Wiss. 1893. This happens because man cannot escape the recognition of a highest power. At the same moment that he denies the true God, he adorns himself with a false God. Religion sits too deeply in human nature for this, and the revelation of God speaks too loudly. Even when in some ages religious indifferentism and skepticism seize deeply and far around themselves, such as e.g. in the age of Pericles, of Emperor Augustus, of the Renaissance, and also in this century, nevertheless religion always comes to the surface again. Rather man seizes upon the coarsest superstition than that he can endure, in the long run, naked, cold unbelief. But we can go even further. Not only does atheism in the absolute sense almost never occur, but even in the sense that it would deny a personal God who lays claim to our worship, it is rare. Naturalism, hylozoism, pantheism are undoubtedly directions that repeatedly present themselves. But they are directions, not so much in the religious as in the philosophical domain. They have never arisen of themselves, spontaneously, but owe their origin to criticism of the religious representations among others. They are not dogmatic but critical; and therefore they always serve only for a time and in a limited circle. A people, a society, a church, a congregation of such naturalists and pantheists is unthinkable and impossible; the pantheists themselves acknowledge this, the religious representation is necessary for the lesser man, but only the philosopher elevates himself to the pure concept. From this it follows that faith in a personal God is natural and normal; it arises everywhere spontaneously and in all human beings. But atheism, even as denial of a personal God, is exception. It is philosophy, not religion. There is truth in the sharp word of Schopenhauer: ein unpersönlicher Gott ist gar kein Gott, sondern bloss ein missbrauchtes Wort, ein Unbegriff, eine contradictio in adjecto, ein Shiboleth für Philosophieprofessoren, welche, nachdem sie die Sache haben aufgeben mussen, mit dem Worte durchzuschleichen bemüht sind, Par. u. Parel. I⁵ 123. cf. Welt als Wille u. Vorst. II⁶ 398, 406, 739. Therefore a certain will is also necessary not to believe in a God, in a personal God. Deum non esse non credit, nisi cui Deum non esse expedit. There are no athei who are firmly and certainly, even to martyrdom, convinced of their unbelief. Because it is abnormal and unnatural, resting not on immediate apprehensions but on mediate proofs and fallible reasonings, therefore it is never certain of its cause. The proofs for the existence of a personal God may be weak, they are always still stronger than those for the denial. To prove that there is no God is even impossible; for this one would have to be omniscient and omnipresent, that is, God oneself, cf. Voetius, de atheismo, Disp. I 114-225. G. J. Vossius, de origine et progressu idol. I c. 3. Leydecker, Fax Veritatis III controv. 3. Turretinus, Theol. El. III qu. 2. Maresius, Syst. Theol. 1673 p. 44. Buddeus, Theses theol. de atheismo et superst., ed. Lulofs L. B. 1747. Hodge, Syst. Theol. I 198, 242. Flint, Antith. theories³, lecture I, Hoekstra, Des Christens godsvrucht bl. 6. Doedes, Inl. tot de leer v. God. 57v.


2. This naturalness, universality, and necessity of religion and knowledge of God has already early led to the thought that these were innate and inborn in man by nature. The fact remains that all human beings from earliest youth have consciousness not only of a physical but also of a psychical, spiritual, invisible world. True and false, good and evil, right and wrong, beautiful and ugly are indeed no weighable and measurable quantities and cannot be perceived with the five senses, but they are a reality that stands for our consciousness much more firmly than that of matter and force. Materialism may reckon only with gravity and heat and electricity; but faith and hope and love are entirely different forces that have governed humanity and preserved it from sinking into bestiality. Augustine has rightly said that the truth of spiritual things is actually much more certain and firm than that of visible things. Nihil absurdius dici potest, quam ea esse quae oculis videmus, ea vero non esse quae intelligentia cernimus, cum dubitare dementis sit intelligentiam incomparabiliter oculis anteferri, de immort. an. c. 10 n. 17. de civ. XIX c. 18. The truths of mathesis, of logika, the fundamental principles of ethics, law, religion stand indubitably firm for all. They bear a character of naturalness, universality, and necessity that cannot be denied by anyone. They appear as ideae innatae enclosed in human nature, given with his birth.

The doctrine of innate concepts already has its root in Greek philosophy. There it presented a great problem for thought, how learning is possible at all. For one of two things seemed to be the case: either we already know something and then we cannot learn it anymore, or we do not know it, but how then is it to be explained that we strive after it in order to learn it, Zeller, Philos. d. Gr. I⁴ 996, II⁴ 823, III 189. Plato solved this problem through his doctrine of recollection; the soul had beheld the ideas in all their beauty before its connection with the body and preserved the images thereof deeply in its memory. He proved this especially with mathematics, which we can construct entirely from our mind without the aid of sense-perception, but otherwise believed that all learning presupposed the pre-existence of the soul, Zeller, ib. II⁴ 639, 643 f. 823 f. Aristotle indeed considered sense-perception the way to knowledge, but nevertheless judged that reason brought along potentially some highest, general principles that stand firm by themselves, lie at the basis of all proofs, and are recognized by all, ib. III 188 f. The Stoics spoke of κοιναι ἐννοιαι, φυσικαι ἐννοιαι, ἐμφυτοι προληψεις, i.e., such concepts which by virtue of the nature of our thinking are derived by all from perception, ib. IV³ 74 f. 389 f. With all these philosophers there is as yet no question of innate ideas in the strict sense. Plato does not limit recollection to a few innate concepts but extends it to all knowledge; and Aristotle and others do speak of general principles but teach explicitly that these are not given as ideas with birth but are found specifically through the medium of perception and thought, but in such a way that every normal human being must then find them. In the proper sense the doctrine of innate concepts first appears with Cicero. He speaks of notiones impressae, cogitationes insitae, innatae and assumes a knowledge of all sorts of truths before all experience and investigation. According to him there are semina innata virtutum, Tusc. III 1, 2, notitiae parvae rerum maximarum, which nature plants in our soul sine doctrina, de fin. V 21, 59, an innate knowledge of God, Deos esse natura opinamur, Tusc. I 16, 36. Nat. D. I 1, 2 etc. Zeller, IV³ 659 f.


3. In modern philosophy the doctrine of ideae innatae was advanced by Cartesius, who also was the first to use this term and thus began to use idea in a sense unusual up to that time. With Cartesius this doctrine was connected with his dualism between soul and body. The cognitio intellectualis cannot be derived from sense-perception; this gives only the occasio, by which our mind forms the representations and concepts per innatam facultatem, Renati Descartes, Notae p. 185. The cognitio proceeds from a principle of its own, i.e., from ideae innatae. Among these ideae innatae that of God is the foremost, which is, as it were, a nota artificis, operi suo impressa. Medit. tertia p. 24. But he conceives the being-innate of these ideas in such a way that the soul possesses by nature the power, the facultas, the dispositio to produce them from itself. The ideas are thus not actual but potential in our mind, Object. et Respons. p. 102, Notae p. 184, 185. Also according to Leibniz the necessary and universal truths do not come to us from without, but they proceed from ourselves, such as e.g. those of substance, duration, change, cause, mathematical truths and especially also the idea of God. Together these truths form the natural light of reason. But the being-innate of these truths is explained by Leibniz more extensively and clearly than by Cartesius. The latter said only that those ideas were potential in our mind. Leibniz however says that they are virtualiter innate, comme des inclinations, des dispositions, des habitudes ou des virtualités naturelles et non pas comme des actions. The mind of man has not merely the receptivity to know them, for then all knowledge could be called innate, but it is the source of these truths, it can bring them forth from itself, les tirer lui-même de son fond. The ideas thus lie, as it were, preformed in the soul of man. And this is possible, because there is a representing and thinking without consciousness. They come to consciousness when sense-perception gives occasion to it. This brings the ideas virtually present in us also actually into our mind, cf. Spruĳt, Proeve van de leer der aangeb. begrippen 144, Stöckl, Gesch. d. n. Phil. I 426, Malebranche believed that learning and knowing in man could be explained in no other way than by the assumption that we see the ideas in God and that God is thus immediately present in our understanding as the universal and infinite being. Following in his footsteps the ontologism of Gioberti, Gratry, Ubaghs and others taught that we immediately behold God in our mind as the absolute being and that thus an intuitive knowledge of God is present in man, Stöckl, Gesch. d. neuern Philos. II 568 f. 620 f. By Kant this doctrine of innate concepts was significantly modified. Following the terminology of Wolff he spoke of knowledge apriori and aposteriori, and now taught no innate concepts but rather innate forms, namely forms of intuition, i.e. space and time, forms of understanding, i.e. categories, and forms of reason, i.e. the ideas God, virtue and immortality. The idealism of Fichte and Hegel pushed this doctrine to such an extreme that they construed not only the knowledge of necessary and universal truths but all knowledge, and indeed all being, the entire material world from thinking. The grounds on which this doctrine of ideae innatae mainly rests are these: Learning, the ability to learn, presupposes that what is learned is already present in some manner in our mind. Reasoning and proof are built upon principia that must stand firm per se and apriori. Experience gives only δοξα, contingent truths; universal and necessary truths can proceed only from the mind of man itself. That there are such universal and necessary truths is proven by the consensus gentium. And above all, the opposition between soul and body is of such a nature that representations and concepts cannot have their origin in sense-perception; they must be explained either from the mind of man or from the Spirit of God, in whom man beholds all ideas. On the other hand the doctrine of innate concepts was combated by the Socinians, who rejected natural religion, Fock, Der Socin. 307 f., cf. also Episcopius, Inst. theol. I c. 3, Locke, Essay concerning human understanding I ch. 2-4, Hobbes, de cive c. 14 n. 19 etc. They relied upon the following considerations: The doctrine of ideae innatae is completely superfluous, because the origin of these ideas can also be well explained in another way. History teaches that no single representation and no single concept is the same among all human beings and peoples. Even innate moral principles do not exist; over good and evil the greatest possible difference prevails; and the concept of God is so little innate that there even exist atheistic human beings and peoples. Children, idiots, the insane therefore know nothing of such innate concepts. All knowledge in man stems from sense-perception; nihil est in intellectu quod non prius fuerit in sensu. This combatting of the ideae innatae made great progress in England and France in the last century. The materialism of this century joined itself thereto. The doctrine of innate concepts has been generally abandoned and replaced by the theory of the inheritance of properties, Darwin, Afst. v. d. mensch, hoofdst. 3, 4. Spencer, bĳ Spruyt Proeve 342.
