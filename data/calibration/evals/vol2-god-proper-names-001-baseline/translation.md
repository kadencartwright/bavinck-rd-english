§ 25. The Proper Names of God.


1. Among the names of God, those names clearly distinguish themselves in the first place by which He is indicated, designated, and addressed as a personal being in distinction from all creatures. All languages possess such names for the Divine Being. Although God has no name in Himself, we have need to indicate Him and have no other means for this than a name. *Nisi enim nomen scieris, rerum cognitio perit*, Isidorus apud Moor I 504. The Greek word θεος, formerly derived from τιθεναι, θεειν, θεασθαι, Suicerus, s.v., M. Vitringa I 134, is nowadays connected by some philologists with Ζευς, Διος, Jupiter, Deus, Diana, Juno, Dio, Dieu; then it is identical with the Sanskrit name dêva, bright heaven, and derived from the stem div, to shine, to glitter. The interchange of the designation heaven and God occurs in many languages; the oldest Greek god was called Uranus, certainly identical with the Sanskrit name Varuna; Taengri among the Tartars and Turks, Thian among the Chinese indicates both heaven and God; and also in Scripture the name God is replaced by that of heaven, e.g., in the expression kingdom of heaven or God's. Another Greek name is δαιμων, from the verb δαιω, and indicates God as distributor and disposer of fate; on the other hand, κυριος, from κυρος, [means] the mighty one, the lord, the owner, the ruler. Our word God is of uncertain origin. It has been connected with the word good; with the Zend khodâ, *a se datus, per se ipsum existens*; with the Sanskrit gudha or gutha, κευδω, which would indicate God as the hidden one, or with a stem, κοδω, κοσμος, which would mean to arrange, to order, or with the Aryan çuddhas, pure, good, etc., but all these derivations are uncertain. The name Asura among the Indians, and Ahura among the Persians indicates God as the living one. Cf. Kluge, *Etymol. Wörterbuch der deutschen Sprache*, s. v. Gott. Cremer, *Wört. der neut. Gr.* s.v. Herzog² 5, 290. Hoekstra, *Wijs. Godsd.* I 309 p.


2. Scripture speaks many times of the name of God in a very broad sense. The Jews therefore counted no fewer than seventy of them, Eisenmenger, *Entdecktes Judenthum* I 455; and in Christian theology, His perfections were also at first included among the names of God. But gradually a distinction arose; already Jerome limited the names of God to a tenfold number, namely, El, Elohim, Elohe, Tzebaoth, Eljon, Escher ehje, Adonai, Iah, Ihvh, Schaddai, and was followed therein by many, Alsted, *Theol. schol.* 71 sq. Moor, I 511. The simplest name by which God is indicated in Holy Scripture is אֵל. There is disagreement concerning the derivation. Lagarde connects the word with the root אֱלִי and the preposition אֶל and thinks that the word indicates God as the one who is the goal and object of human desire. But this derivation is just as improbable as that which connects the word with אֵלָה, the sacred tree. According to the vast majority, the word is derived from the stem אוּל, whether in the meaning of being in front, the first, being lord (Nöldeke), or in that of being strong, mighty (Gesenius), litt. apud M. Vitringa I 132. Moor I 515 sq. Schultz, *Altt. Theol.* 508. Smend, *Altt. Theol.* 26. The name אֱלוֹהַּ plur. אֱלֹהִים is derived from the same stem אוּל or from אָלַה, to terrify, and thus makes God known either as the strong one or as the object of terror and fear. The singular is rarely used and poetic, e.g., Ps. 18:32, Job 3:4; on the other hand, the plural is the ordinary name for God. This plural cannot be explained as a *pluralis majestatis*, because this is nowhere customary in Scripture of God; neither can an indication of the Trinity be seen in it, as has been done by many since Lombardus, *Sent.* I dist. 2, Zanchius, *Op.* I 25. Voetius, *Disp.* V 27. M. Vitringa I 209 sq., for Elohim almost always has the adjective and the verb in singular with it, Augustinus, *de trin.* II 11. Bellarminus, *de Christo*, c. 6. Calvin, *Inst.* I 13, 9. Gomarus, *Theses Theol.*, disp. 5. Moor I 796. Modern critics for the most part see in it a remnant of earlier polytheism, but this explanation not only encounters the same objection as the earlier Trinitarian one, but also, as is evident from later investigations, has been customary outside Israel as a name for one single God, Noordtzij, *Oost. Lichtstralen over West. Schriftbeschouwing* 1897 p. 41 f. Therefore this plural is better understood as a *pluralis abstractus* (Ewald), or as a *pluralis quantitatis*, which, like מַיִם and שָׁמַיִם, indicates an unlimited magnitude (Oehler), or as an intensive plural, which indicates a fullness of power (Delitzsch). Several times Elohim is construed with an adjective or verb in plural, Gen. 20:13, 28:13 f., 35:7, Ex. 32:4, 8, Josh. 24:19, 1 Sam. 4:8, 17:26, 2 Sam. 7:23, 1 Kings 12:28, Ps. 58:12, 121:5, Job 35:10, Jer. 10:10; and a similar plural is also observable in the personal pronoun, Gen. 1:26, 3:22, 11:7, Isa. 6:8, 41:22, in קְדֹשִׁים, Prov. 9:10, Hos. 12:1, עֹשֶׂים, Job 35:10, Isa. 54:5, בֹּרְאִים Eccl. 12:1, Ps. 149:2, and in אֲדֹנָי. All these plural forms indicate God as the fullness of life and power. Durch Elohim wird das göttliche Wesen in seinem Urverhältniss und in seinem stetigen Grundverhältniss zur Welt bezeichnet. Es ist eine Verhältnissbestimmung, nicht eine unmittelbare innere Wesensbestimmung, und zwar ist es Bestimmung des absoluten Hoheitsbegriffs in Bezug auf die ganze Welt, Beck, *Gl.* II 22, cf. litt. apud M. Vitringa I 133, Moor I 518, Oehler, *Theol.* § 36, Schultz, *Altt. Th.* 516. The name עֶלְיוֹן, LXX ὑψιστος, indicates God as the High One, Exalted above all; it occurs in the mouth of Melchizedek, Gen. 14:18, of Balaam, Num. 24:16, of the king of Babylon, Isa. 14:14, cf. also Mark 5:7, Luke 1:32, 35, Acts 16:17 and furthermore especially in poetry. אֲדֹנָי, alternating with הָאָדוֹן, which is again strengthened to אֲדוֹן אֲדֹנִים or אֲדוֹן כָּל הָאָרֶץ, makes God known as the Lord to whom everything is subject and before whom man stands as a servant, Gen. 18:27. In earlier times God was also called בַּעַל in the same sense, Hos. 2:16, but later this name acquired an idolatrous character and therefore fell out of use, Robertson, *Israels oude godsdienst* pp. 200 f. All these names are not yet *nomina propria* in a stricter sense; they are also sometimes used of idols, of men, Gen. 33:10, Ex. 7:1, 4:16, and of authorities, Ex. 12:12, 21:5f., 22:7, Lev. 19:32, Num. 33:4, Judg. 5:8, 1 Sam. 2:25, Ps. 58:2, 82:1, but they are nevertheless the ordinary names by which God is designated and addressed. They are also generally Semitic names, which make God known in His exaltation above all creature. The Semites gladly call God Lord, King; they feel themselves deeply dependent upon Him and bow themselves humbly, as servants, before Him, yet they do not express in these names a philosophical contemplation of the essence of God but place His relation to the creatures, particularly to men, in the foreground.


3. This high, exalted God, however, also descends from His transcendence to the creature. He reveals Himself not only generally through creation to all nations, but has also made Himself known in a particular sense to Israel. The first name by which God now appears in the special revelation is that of שַׁדַּי or אֵל שַׁדַּי. As such God reveals Himself to Abraham, when He constitutes him a father of many nations and seals His covenant with circumcision, Gen. 17:1. In the time of the patriarchs this name therefore occurs repeatedly, Gen. 28:3, 35:11, 43:14, 48:3, 49:25, Ex. 6:3, Num. 24:4, but furthermore also in Job, in several psalms and a few times with the prophets. In the N.T. it is rendered by παντοκρατωρ, 2 Cor. 6:18, Rev. 4:8 etc. The origin of the name is still obscure. Nöldeke has derived it from שַׁד, lord, and punctuated it as שֵׁדַי, but as is evident from Gen. 43:14, 49:25, Ezek. 10:5, the name is undoubtedly an adjective. Formerly it was derived from שְׁ for אֲשֶׁר and דַּי, ἱκανος, and then translated as *qui est sufficiens*, all-sufficient; or from שָׁדַד in the meaning of being strong, devastating; or also from שָׂדָה or אֲשָׁד to pour out, so that God is thereby indicated as the one who communicates everything abundantly. Everywhere, however, with this name the concept of power and invincible strength stands in the foreground, and Isa. 13:6 connects—if only in a play on words—this name with שָׁדַד, to devastate, cf. Joel 1:15. Therefore this name makes God known to us as the one who possesses all power and thus can break all resistance and make everything subservient to His will. While Elohim is the God of creation and of nature, El-Shaddai is the God who subordinates all powers of that nature to and places them in the service of grace, cf. annotation on Gen. 17:1, Zanchius, *Op.* II 43. M. Vitringa I 132. Moor I 522 sq. Oehler, *Theol.* § 37. Delitzsch on Gen. 17:1 etc. In this name the θειοτης and αἰδιος δυναμις of God is no longer an object of terror and fear, but a source of salvation and comfort. God gives Himself to His people and stands with His invincible power for the fulfillment of His promises, for the maintenance of His covenant. Therefore from now on He is also repeatedly called the God of Abraham, Gen. 24:12, Isaac, Gen. 28:13, Jacob, Ex. 3:6, the God of the fathers, Ex. 3:13, 15, the God of the Hebrews, Ex. 3:18, the God of Israel, Gen. 33:20, and with Isaiah often the Holy One of Israel. God is the Exalted One, Creator of heaven and earth, the Almighty, but who at the same time also stands in a special, favorable relation to His people.

As God of grace, however, He appears especially in the name יְהֹוָה. The Jews called this name the name par excellence, the name of essence, the proper name, the glorious name, the name of four letters, τετραγραμματον, etc., and derived from Lev. 24:16 and Ex. 3:15, where one read לְעֹלָם, to conceal, the conclusion that it was forbidden to pronounce it. It is unknown when this opinion arose among the Jews. But it is certain that the LXX already regularly read Adonai and therefore rendered the name by κυριος; other translations followed this example and translated the name by Dominus, The Lord, der Herr, HEERE, Syn. Dordr. sess. 12 etc., French has l'Éternel. Thereby the original, correct pronunciation has also been lost. The church fathers called the name ἀπορρητον, ἀλεκτον, ἀφραστον, probably not because they held the pronunciation of this name to be unlawful, but because the Jews so judged and the pronunciation was actually lost, Moor I 534, Buddeus, *Inst. theol. dogm.* I 188. In Greek the four letters were written as ΠΙΠΙ, or also according to Diodorus Siculus and Origenes rendered by Ιαω or Ιαη, according to Jerome by Jaho, according to Philo Byblius by Ιευω, according to Clemens Alex. by Ιαου. Theodoretus relates that the Jews pronounced Αϊα and the Samaritans Ιαβε. All this points back to an old pronunciation Iahveh, Schultz, *Altt. Theol.*⁴ 523. With appeal to the tradition among the Jews, by some the name was pronounced as Jeve, e.g., by Joachim of Fiore in his *Evangelium aeternum*. Indeed this punctuation is to be found with Samuel b. Meir, and it was later still defended by Hottinger, Reland et al., Delitzsch, *Neuer Comm. über die Genesis* 1887 p. 546 f. The pronunciation Jehovah is of recent date: it gained entrance especially through the Franciscan Petrus Galatinus, who however was contested by many, among others by Genebrardus, Petavius, *de Deo* VIII c. 9, Herzog² 4, 173. Later men such as Drusius, Amama, Scaliger, Vriemoet and others also asserted that the pronunciation Jehovah could not be the correct one, but had borrowed the vowels from Adonai, Voetius, *Disp.* V 55, cf. litt. apud M. Vitringa I 130. Indeed this punctuation is burdened by very serious objections. First, Ihvh in the Hebrew Bible is a *keri perpetuum* and now has the vowels of Adonai, then those of Elohim; furthermore, the form Jehovah is un-Hebraic and inexplicable; and finally this punctuation dates from a time when the Jews had long considered the pronunciation of the name forbidden. If this vocalization is now incorrect, the question arises how then the name is to be explained. The assertion that it is of Egyptian origin (Voltaire, Schiller, Wegscheider, Heeren, Brugsch), is contradicted by Ex. 5:2 and finds almost no defense anymore. Also the opinion of Hartmann, Bohlen, Colenso, Dozy, Land, that it is Canaanite or Phoenician and was adopted by the Israelites after their entrance into Canaan, has proven untenable and has been sufficiently refuted, Kuenen, *G. v. I.* I 397-401. Valeton, *De Isr. Godsnamen*, *Theol. Stud.* VII 1889 pp. 173-221, especially pp. 176 f. The Israelite origin of the name may thus be regarded as established. Then, however, there is also no doubt possible, or it comes from the stem הוה or היה, and there is, since the pronunciation of Jaho, advocated by von Hartmann, *Religionsphilos.* I 370 f., cannot be considered, Kuenen, *Volksgodsd. en Wereldgodsd.* 261 f., only still disagreement over whether it is a 3rd person impf. of kal or of hiphil. The latter explanation is defended by Gesenius, Schrader, Lagarde, Schultz, Land, Kuenen, *G. v. I.* I 275, really only on the ground that such an exalted concept of God as would express itself in the kal-form was not yet conceivable in the time of Moses. The name Jahveh would then not mean He who is, but He who causes to be, who gives life, Creator. But Smend, *Altt. Rel. Gesch.* 21 says rightly from his standpoint that also this name is still much too high for that time; and he calls this explanation also therefore improbable, because the hiphil of the verb הוה never occurs. Thus only the derivation remains which is given in Ex. 3 of the name. Yet there is still disagreement then about the meaning of the name. The church fathers thought with this name especially of the *aseitas*; God was the being, the eternal, immutable being over against the οὐκ Ὀν of the idols and the μη ὀν of the creatures. Others, such as W. R. Smith, Smend loc. cit. p. 21, appeal to Ex. 3:12, and interpret the name as He who will be *with you*. Both interpretations are unacceptable; the latter, because the addition *with you*, עִמָּך, could not then be absent, and the former, because it sounds too philosophical and also finds no support in Ex. 3. For in verses 13-15 the meaning of the name is clearly indicated. In full it reads אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה; and therewith the Lord says that He who now calls Moses and wants to save His people is the same as He who appeared to their fathers. He is who He is, yesterday and today the same in eternity. This meaning is set forth more closely in verse 15: Ihvh, the God of your fathers, the God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob sends Moses, and this is His name forever. God does not here call Himself the being without further qualification and does not give an explanation of His *aseitas*, but He expressly says what and how He is. How and what shall He then be? That is not to be said in one word, that is not to be defined by a single attribute, but He shall be who He shall be. Everything is enclosed therein; general and indefinite is this attribute but therefore also so rich and deep in meaning. He shall be who He has been for the patriarchs, who He now is, who He shall remain; He shall be everything for His people. It is no new and no strange God who comes to them through Moses, but it is the God of the fathers, the unchangeable, the constant, the faithful, the always self-same, who does not abandon or forsake His people, but always seeks it out again and saves it, unchangeable in His grace, in His love, in His help, who shall be what He is, because He is always Himself; אֲנִי הוּא He therefore calls Himself in Isaiah, the first and the last, chapters 41:4, 43:10, 13, 25, 44:6, 48:12. Naturally the *aseitas* lies here indeed at the foundation, but this nevertheless does not come to the foreground and is not expressed directly in the name. From this explanation it also appears whether and to what extent the name Ihvh was already known before the time of Moses. Ex. 6:3 does not say that the name as name was then communicated to Moses, but that the Lord was not known to the fathers with or in respect to His name. The name is therefore already found repeatedly before Ex. 6, occurs in many proper names, such as Jochebed, Ahijah, Abijah, 1 Chron. 2:24, 25, and could therefore also not be an entirely new name, because Moses, in order to find hearing with his people, had to appear precisely not with a strange name but in the name of the God of the fathers, Ex. 3:12. The intention of Ex. 6:3 can therefore be none other than that the sense and meaning of this name was first made known to Moses by the Lord Himself then. And this is indeed also so. Only in Ex. 3 does the Lord Himself give an explanation of this name; here He says how He wants this name to be understood. The name already existed earlier, was also already employed several times by the Lord Himself then, Gen. 15:7, 28:13, and used as a name of address, Gen. 14:22, 24:3, 28:16, 15:2, 8, 32:9. But nowhere is an explanation of that name given. It is quite possible in the abstract that the name Ihvh originally, according to its derivation, meant something entirely different than is said in Ex. 3. Ex. 3 gives no etymology, no word-explanation, but an explanation of the matter. Just as God in His special revelation to Israel took over all kinds of religious customs, circumcision, sabbath, offering, priesthood, etc., and gave them a special meaning, so He has also done with this name. Abstracting from origin and original meaning, the Lord says in Ex. 6 how and in what sense He is Iahveh, the I shall be who I shall be. The name Ihvh is from now on the description and the guarantee of this, that God is and remains the God of His people, unchangeable in His grace and faithfulness. And that could also only now, in Moses' time, come out. Just a long time had to elapse in order to prove that God was faithful and unchangeable; one's faithfulness can only in the long run and especially in times of distress be tested. So it was with Israel. Centuries had elapsed after the time of the patriarchs; Israel was oppressed and sat down in misery. And now God says: I am who I am, Iahveh, the unchangeably faithful, the God of the fathers, the God also now still of you and unto eternity. Now God lays in an old name an entirely new meaning, which also can only now first be understood by the people. And therefore Ihvh is Israel's God from the land of Egypt, Hos. 12:9, 13:4.
