# Reviewer Findings

## Summary

The translation successfully preserves the required Greek, Hebrew, Latin, and German spans and adheres to the provided glossary, but it critically fails to translate several Dutch terms and abbreviations—most notably 'interpunctie', 'vocalisatie', 'o. a.', and 'e. a.'—leaving them as untranslated artifacts in the English prose. While the formal theological tone is generally maintained, these lapses constitute a significant corruption of the target language.

## Findings

- [high] preserved-language-corruption: The Dutch words 'interpunctie' (pointing/vocalization) and 'vocalisatie' (vocalization) appear untranslated in the main text of paragraph 3, creating incoherent English prose.
- [medium] prose-quality: Dutch abbreviations 'o. a.' (onder andere/among others) and 'e. a.' (en anderen/et al.) are preserved in the translation instead of being rendered into standard English scholarly abbreviations or phrases.
- [low] prose-quality: Dutch biblical abbreviations and spellings such as 'Jes.' and 'Jesaia' are retained instead of being normalized to the standard English forms 'Isa.' and 'Isaiah'.

## Recommended Follow-Up

- Replace 'interpunctie' with 'vocalization' or 'pointing' and 'vocalisatie' with 'vocalization'.
- Expand 'o. a.' to 'among others' or 'e.g.' and 'e. a.' to 'et al.' or 'and others'.
- Standardize biblical names and abbreviations to their English equivalents (e.g., 'Isaiah', 'Isa.').
