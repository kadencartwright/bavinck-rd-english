§ 24. CLASSIFICATION OF THE NAMES OF GOD.


1. Holy Scripture calls God by many names, but never proceeds from an abstract concept of God. Never does it elevate one attribute of God at the expense of the other. To be sure, now one attribute, then another, comes to the fore, but between all attributes there is perfect harmony. It is the intention of Scripture to bring all virtues of God equally to her honor. Even as the person of Christ does not represent a determinate one-sided character or temperament and yet is a person full of life and reality, so also God in His revelation is always unfolding all His virtues in harmonious connection. Nowhere is there mention of the being of God in abstracto. The Hebrew word ‎‏תּוּשִׁיָּה‏‎ from the root ‎‏יָשָׁה‏‎; existence, being, Arab. III to help, to raise up, indicates that which has permanence, is enduring, provides benefit, in concrete especially true wisdom and happiness, Job 5:12, 6:13, 12:16, 26:3 (30:22), Prov. 2:7, 3:21, 8:14 and further only Isa. 28:29, Mic. 6:9, but in none of these places does it stand for the being of God, cf. Delitzsch on Job 5:12. Neither can the New Testament words θεοτης Col. 2:9, θειοτης Rom. 1:29, μορφη θεου Phil. 2:6, θεια φυσις 2 Petr. 1:4 cf. Gal. 4:8 prove that there is spoken there of the being or nature of God in distinction from His attributes, as Polanus would have it, Synt. theol. p. 135. God’s being becomes knowable to us in His revelation, that is, in His names. The names of God are designations of His ἀρεται, 1 Petr. 2:9. This usage of language connects with Isa. 42:8, 12. 43:21. 63:5, where the Hebrew ‎‏תְּהִּלָה‏‎ praise, honor, in the LXX is translated by ἀρετη, cf. Hab. 3:3. Zech. 6:13. The church has the calling to proclaim God’s virtues, that is, to give Him honor for the δοξα displayed in all His works. Of a description of God’s being, apart from the attributes, Holy Scripture never speaks. In the earliest period something similar was not attempted in Christian theology either. Under the nomina Dei everything that one had to say of God was comprehended. The names of God were indication not only of the nomina propria but also of what were later called attributes and even of the persons in the Divine being, Clemens Alex. Strom. V 12. Augustine, de doctr. chr. I 5. Pseudo-Dion., de div. nom. c. 1 sq. Damascene, de fide orth. I c. 12. Thomas, S. Theol. I qu. 13. Catech. Rom. I c. 2 qu. 11. 13. Kleutgen, Theol. der Vorzeit I² S. 211 f. Gerhard, Loci Theol. loc. 2. Hyperius, Meth. Theol. p. 89 sq. c. 3. Polanus, Synt. Theol. Lib. 2 c. 6. Zanchius, Op. II col. 9. 10. Walaeus, Op. I p. 152 sq. Alsted, Theol. did. schol. p. 61. The attributes were therefore immediately included in the idea of God; thus for example in Irenaeus, adv. haer. I c. 14. II c. 13., 35 etc. Augustine indeed speaks of God’s essentia, but he understands thereby the fullness of God’s being and immediately includes all attributes, simplicity, eternity, goodness, wisdom etc. therein. In the confessions it is also often spoken of God in that manner, without distinction of being and attributes, and without classification of the attributes, Conc. Lateran. in Denzinger, Enchir. symb. n. 355, Conc. Vatic. de fide c. 1. Conf. August. art. 1. Conf. Gall. art. 1. Scot. art. 1. Belg. art. 1. Conf. Westm. cap. 2. And also later various theologians do not enter into these distinctions and treat the attributes without previously speaking of the nature of God, for example Thomas. S. Theol. I qu. 3 sq. Ursinus, Tract. theol. p. 46-70.


2. But soon a distinction arose. One was led thereto by the question, what was the attribute which distinguished God immediately from all creatures, what was the principal concept from which one therefore had to proceed in the doctrine of God. Now already Platonic philosophy had sought that principal concept in being, and Philo had connected this with the name Ihvh, the only name which indicated not an operation or power but the being of God Himself, and therefore called God often ὁ ὠν or το ὀν, Zeller, Philos. V³ 356. This description of God’s being was taken up in Christian theology. Irenaeus describes God often as absolutely simple, adv. haer. II c. 13. 28 IV 11 and calls Him even inexcogitabilis et insubstantivus, I c. 14, but against Gnosticism lays emphasis especially on this, that God is the creator of all things and that He has revealed Himself in His works, Harnack D. G. I 485 f. On the other hand, in Origen, de princ. I 1 sq. c. Cels. VI 64. VII 42-51, Athanasius, de decr. nic. syn. c. 11, Damascene, de fide orth. I c. 2. 4. 9. etc. cf. Harnack D. G. II 119 f. Schwane, Dogm. I 132 II 35, Kattenbusch, Vergl. Confessionskunde I 310 f., God is the one, the being, indeed the elevated above all being, ὁ ὠν, ὁ ὠν ¡καθ’ἑαυτον, το ὀν, who is being itself, who has being from and through Himself. And in the footsteps of Philo this was all derived from or tied to the name of Ihvh in the Old Testament. In the West these determinations were taken over. Augustine describes God repeatedly as summum esse, summum bonum, verum, pulchrum etc. God is a substantia, for what is no substance does not exist, Enarr. in Ps. 67. But, because the word substance is often used in contrast with accidentia, which inhere in the substance and have it as subject, Augustine prefers to use of God the word essentia, quam Graeci οὐσιαν vocant and for which also the word natura is often used, de trin. V 2 VII 5 sq. de civ. XII, 2. de doctr. chr. I 6. For with God there is no distinction between substance and accidents; His being is no bearer of attributes, but these are identical with the being itself. God is the highest, best, most beautiful, most perfect being, quo esse aut cogitari melius nihil possit, de lib. arb. II 6. de doctr. chr. I 7. He is Deus, supra quem nihil, extra quem nihil, sine quo nihil est; summa vita, summa veritas, summa beatitudo, summa sapientia, summa essentia, de civ. XII 2. Solil. I 1. And Augustine also appeals for this description of God to the name Ihvh, de trin. V 2. VII 5. de doctr. chr. I 32. We find this same description then repeatedly later, in Hilary, de trin. I c. 1 sq. Pseudo-Dion., de div. nom. cap. 1. § 6, who however cap. 4 § 1 proceeds from the idea of the good and c. 5 § 1 says, that this extends itself further than that of being, Anselm, Monol. c. 28. Prosl. c. 17 sq. Lombard, Sent. I dist. 8. Thomas, S. Theol. I qu. 2 art. 3 and qu. 3. S. c. Gent. I c. 16 sq. Bonaventure, Brevil. I cap. 2. Sent. I dist. 8. etc. Petavius, Theol. lib. 1 c. 6. Perrone, Prael. theol. II 81-90. C. Pesch, Prael. II 46-70. Jansen II p. 26-46. Although this now indeed became the common view, there were nevertheless those who chose another starting point. They sought the principal concept in the idea of God not in absolute being, in the esse a se (aseity), but in infinity; and they therefore described the being of God preferably as an ens infinitum. Thus did Duns Scotus, who asserted that being could be attributed univoce, in equal sense, to God and to the creature, but that the distinguishing characteristic between God and the creature lay therein, that God was an ens infinitum and creatures were finite, Sent. I dist. 3 qu. 1. and dist. 8 qu. 3. Furthermore there were still some Thomists, who thought that the proper being of God lay in the intellectual nature and therefore described God as ens intelligens, such as for example Gotti, cf. Perrone, Prael. theol. II p. 82. Kleutgen, Theol. der Vorzeit I² 229.


3. The Reformation brought no change in this view. One joined oneself to the one or the other description. The Reformed at least in the earliest period mostly took over the description of Augustine and Thomas. They proceeded from the concept of aseity or independence and often described God as an ens independens, Zwingli, Op. III 155 sq. IV 81 sq. Hyperius, Meth. Theol. p. 87. Martyr, Loci Comm. p. 39b. Polanus Synt. Theol. lib. II c. 5. Zanchius, Op. II col. 49 sq. Ursinus, Tract. theol. 1584 p. 46 sq. Besides these there also occur descriptions of God as spiritus increatus, spiritus simplicissimus, spiritus a se ipso existens, sometimes still supplemented with the trinitarian formula unus essentia, trinus personis, Heppe, Dogm. der ev. ref. K. S. 35 f. Schweizer, Gl. der ev. ref. K. I 244 f. The Lutherans used the definition essentia spiritualis infinita, ens spirituale a se subsistens, spiritus independens, Schmid, Dogm. der ev. luth. K⁶. S. 73-77. Hase, Hutt. red. § 54. In contrast Socinianism took another position. Setting aside all metaphysical questions, it laid emphasis only on the will of God. The knowledge of God consists only in the knowledge of His will. Religion loses all mysticism and passes over into worship. God withdraws more and more from world and humanity. In Remonstrantism, rationalism and English deism this is continued, cf. above p. 15. 16. Against this cold, moralistic concept of God came again reaction from the side of philosophy. Spinoza seized upon the idea of being again, conceived God as substantia unica, infinita, necessario existens, ens absolute infinitum, absolute causa prima et immanens, Eth. I prop. 7 sq. and spoke again of a love of God, amor Dei intellectualis, which granted the highest blessedness, Eth. V prop. 15 sq. Although rationalism and deism remained dominant even after Spinoza, yet around the middle of the 18th century one became more and more weary of this. The great minds, Goethe, Lessing, Herder felt themselves attracted to Spinoza, and soon his pantheism made entry into philosophy. Kant already undermined the foundations of rationalism, although he still called himself a deist, Drews, Die deutsche Spekul. I 104. Fichte undertook the struggle against the concepts God and immortality as products of eudaemonism. In his exaggerated moralism God was equal to him to the pure ego, to the moral world-order; God was no being, no substance but absolutes Thun, Thätigkeit; thus in his Wissenschaftslehre 1794, Ueber den Grund unseres Glaubens an eine göttliche Weltregierung 1798, Appellation an das Publikum 1799. Later he partially retreated from this and joined himself more closely to Spinoza, Anweisung zum seligen Leben oder Religionslehre 1806. Schleiermacher showed still stronger agreement with Spinoza. God and world are correlata, Dialektik S. 162. God is the Woher unseres empfänglichen und selbsthätigen Daseins, Glaub. I⁵ S. 20. Schelling distinguished himself from both thereby, that he reckoned not only with the spiritual, religion, morals but also with objective nature. It was his goal to lift natural science from the deplorable condition in which it found itself. And thus he conceives then both, nature and spirit together; nature is visible spirit, spirit invisible nature; he sees in both a continuous, organic revelation of the Absolute, which is elevated above all oppositions, one, simple, eternal, without predicates, unity of the infinite and the finite, of God and world, ἑν και παν, Drews, Die deutsche Spekul. I S. 201 f. Finally Hegel transformed this system into a logical idealism. Nature and history are a logically necessary self-unfolding of the idea. Everything is rational, everything is embodied thought. Reason is thus the absolute substance, it is God Himself. For God is nothing other than the one, living world-idea developing to self-consciousness. God is thus in Hegel indeed reason, thought, spirit, subject, but not in the sense that He would have a proper life for and outside the world. Ohne Welt ist Gott nicht Gott, the world is an essential moment in the life of God, Drews, Die deutsche Spekul. I 229 f.
