§ 22. THE KNOWABILITY OF GOD. (Cognitio Dei acquisita.)

1. Between the cognitio Dei insita and acquisita there may be no antithesis, yet undoubtedly there is a distinction between the two. Often this distinction is conceived such that the first indicates the knowledge of God which is innate to man and accrues to him from his own being, and the second that which, for the increase and extension of the first, comes to man partly from without, from the contemplation of the world. But the difference between the two is not thereby indicated clearly and precisely. For in the proper sense there is no knowledge innate, neither of God nor of the world. All knowledge comes from without into man’s consciousness. What is innate is only the capacity for knowledge; but this capacity first comes to action and deed through the operation of the world in and around us. The seed of religion indeed lies in man, but it germinates only in the field of all human life. As the eye is brought along with man at his birth but sees objects only by the light of the sun, so the believer beholds God first in all the works of his hands. Furthermore, it has become clear to us in the previous § that Christian theology has never understood by the innate and inborn character of the knowledge of God that man brought along any conscious knowledge of God at birth; but it thereby indicated only that the knowledge of God need not be brought to man by force or violence, nor also by logical reasoning or compelling proofs, but that it is natural, spontaneous, and proper to man of itself. A person, developing normally, comes without force or effort, spontaneously to some knowledge of God. Therefore the cognitio Dei insita does not stand in antithesis to the acquisita, for in a broader sense the former may also be called acquisita. To the cognitio Dei, both insita and acquisita, the revelation of God precedes, namely. He does not leave himself unwitnessed. He works with his eternal power and divinity from all sides upon man, not only from without but also from within; in nature and human world, in heart and conscience, in prosperity and adversity God as it were meets man. And he received as created according to God’s image the capacity to catch the impressions thereof and thereby to come to some awareness and some knowledge of the Eternal Being. The cognitio insita, as soon as it is cognitio and thus not only potentia but actus, never arises otherwise than under the operation of the revelation of God in and outside man and is thus insofar always acquisita. For these reasons the distinction between the cognitio Dei insita and acquisita was formerly usually indicated otherwise. It consisted in these two things. First, in this: that the cognitio Dei innata was obtained spontaneously, without effort or compulsion, whereas the cognitio Dei acquisita on the other hand was acquired by reasoning and argument, by reflection and proof along the viae causalitatis, eminentiae and negationis; the former was noetica, the latter dianoetica. And secondly, this difference also followed from it: that the first consisted only in principles and was general and necessary, but the second was more elaborated and developed, furnished more concrete propositions and was therefore also subject to all kinds of doubt and dispute. That there is a God, stood and stands fast for nearly all. But the proofs for the existence of God are found by thinking and are therefore alternately despised and highly esteemed, Moor, Comm. I 41. 44. And therewith the distinction is rightly indicated. It lies not therein, that the innate knowledge of God would arise from man and the acquired from the world. Also the moral proof is derived from the moral consciousness, which is found within man. In both it is the same and the entire revelation of God, from which the cognitio Dei penetrates into our consciousness. But in the cognitio Dei insita that revelation works upon the consciousness of man and cultivates there impressions and realizations. In the cognitio Dei acquisita that revelation of God is thought through by man; his understanding goes to work, his reflection awakens, and with clear, lucid consciousness he seeks by reasoning and proof to climb up from the creatures to God. For man has in no domain of knowledge enough of impressions and realizations. He is not satisfied with consciousness alone. It is not sufficient for him _that_ he knows. He wants also _to know_ that he knows; he seeks to explain the _how_ and _why_ of his knowing. And hence, that the ordinary, everyday, empirical knowing always seeks to pass over into the proper, scientific knowing; that faith elevates itself to theology, and the cognitio Dei insita completes itself in the cognitio Dei acquisita.

2. The division into cognitio Dei insita and acquisita is usually applied only to the theologia naturalis. And this is then distinguished from and often opposed to the theologia revelata. Already earlier this view has been extensively contested, I 219–244. There is no separate theologia naturalis, which could be obtained by man outside all revelation from the thinking contemplation of the world. That knowledge of God, which is summarized in the theologia naturalis, is no product of human reason. It presupposes first of all that God himself reveals himself in the works of his hands; it is not man who seeks God, but God who seeks man also through his works in nature. Furthermore, it is not man who understands and knows this revelation of God by the natural light of reason; all religions of the Gentiles are positive; but on man’s side there is need of a sanctified understanding and an opened eye to observe God, the true and living God, in his creatures. And even this is not yet enough. Also the believer, also the Christian would not understand God’s revelation in nature and could not represent it purely, if God himself had not described in his Word how and what He reveals of himself in all the world. The cognitio Dei naturalis is included in Scripture itself and therein extensively unfolded. Therefore it is also a wholly wrong method, that the Christian in treating the theologia naturalis as it were rids himself of the special revelation in Scripture and of the illumination of the Holy Spirit, discusses it without any Christian Voraussetzung, and thereafter proceeds to the theologia revelata. The Christian stands also as dogmatician from the beginning with both feet on the foundation of the special revelation; not first in the locus de Christo but also in the locus de Deo he is a Christian believer. But standing thus, he looks around him, and armed with the spectacles of Holy Scripture he sees in all the world a revelation of that same God, whom he in Christ knows and confesses as his Father in the heavens. Then, however, under the cognitio Dei insita and acquisita is not to be understood that knowledge which we obtain from creation outside the special revelation. From our earliest youth God’s special revelation in Christ has worked upon us all; we are born in the covenant of grace and thus as Christians; all kinds of Christian influences have formed the knowledge of God, which is now our portion, in much greater measure than the operation which has proceeded from God’s revelation in nature upon our heart; and in the light of that special revelation we have all also learned to contemplate nature and the world around us. We all owe the knowledge of God from nature to God’s special revelation in his Word. Nature would let us hear just as confused voices as the Gentiles, if we had not hearkened to God’s speech in the works of grace and thereby now understood his voice also in the works of nature. But now that revelation of God in nature is also of high value. It is the same God, who speaks to us in nature and in grace, in creation and recreation, in the Logos and in Christ, in the Spirit of God and in the Spirit of Christ. Nature and grace are no antithesis; we have one God, from whom and through whom and to whom they both are.

3. This applies to both the cognitio Dei insita and acquisita; we owe both to Holy Scripture. It is true that it takes no trouble to prove the existence of God. It proceeds from that existence and presupposes that man knows and acknowledges God. It considers man not yet so deeply sunken that he has need of a proof beforehand in order to believe. For he is still bearer of God’s image, God’s offspring, and endowed with a νους, to observe in the creation God’s eternal power and divinity. The denial of God’s existence is in the eye of Scripture a sign of foolishness, of deep moral decay, Ps. 14:2. Such people are exception, no rule. Generally Scripture reckons with such as acknowledge the existence of God spontaneously and by virtue of the prompting of their nature. It appeals not to the reasoning understanding, but to the rational and moral consciousness. It analyzes and argues not, but lets God be seen in all the works of his hands. But that it does then indeed. Heaven and earth together with all creatures, leaf and grass, rain and drought, fruitful and unfruitful years, food and drink, health and sickness, riches and poverty, and all things speak to the pious of God. There is no part, no piece of the world, in which his power and divinity is not beheld. To heaven and earth, birds and ants, flowers and lilies is man referred, to behold and acknowledge God. Scripture reasons not in the abstract and makes God not the conclusion of a syllogism, leaving it now to us whether we shall find that proof compelling. But it speaks with authority, it proceeds theologically and religiously from God, it shows his virtues in his works and it demands that we shall acknowledge God. An ox knows its owner, and an ass the crib of its lord, but Israel has no knowledge, my people understands not, Isa. 1:3. It stands fast for her that God reveals himself in the creatures and that He does not leave himself unwitnessed to anyone, Acts 14:17, Rom. 1:19. And thus appealing to all creation, as a testimony and revelation of God, it contains in germ everything which later was broadly elaborated in the proofs and dialectically unfolded. There is truth in the observation of C. J. Nitzsch, Syst. der Chr. Lehre, 6te Aufl. S. 142, that Holy Scripture gives us a beginning and analogy of the aitiological proof in Rom. 1:20, of the teleological in Ps. 8; 19; Acts 14:17, of the moral in Rom. 2:14 and of the ontological in Acts 17:24, Rom. 1:19, 32. But speaking thus, not in dialectical argument but in the language of testimony, not appealing to the reasoning understanding but making appeal to heart and conscience, to all the rational and moral consciousness of man, Scripture has never been without power and influence. Also in this it is a powerful word, sharp as a two-edged sharp sword, piercing to the division of soul and spirit, a judge of the thoughts and intents of the heart. Also without logical argument and philosophical proof is Scripture in this its testimony powerful, because it is the word of God and finds support in the rational and moral nature of every man. It is God himself, who does not leave himself unwitnessed to anyone. And it is man himself, who, although he was created according to God’s image, must against his will give heed and assent to this testimony. In this light must also the so-called proofs for God’s existence be regarded. That will preserve us both from overestimation and from contempt of the proofs.
