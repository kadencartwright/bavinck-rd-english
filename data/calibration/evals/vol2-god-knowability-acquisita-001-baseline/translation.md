§ 22. THE KNOWABILITY OF GOD. (Cognitio Dei acquisita.)


1. Between the cognitio Dei insita and acquisita there may be no opposition, yet there is undoubtedly a distinction between the two. Often this distinction is conceived such that the first indicates the knowledge of God which is innate to man and belongs to him from his own being, and the second that which, for the increase and extension of the first, comes to man partly from without, from the contemplation of the world. But thereby the difference between the two is not indicated clearly and precisely. For, in the proper sense, there is no innate knowledge, neither of God nor of the world. All knowledge comes from without into man’s consciousness. What is innate is only the faculty of knowledge; but this faculty comes first to action and deed through the operation of the world in and around us. The seed of religion indeed lies in man, but it germinates only in the field of all human life. As the eye is brought along with man at his birth but sees objects only in the light of the sun, so the believer beholds God first in all the works of his hands. Furthermore, it has become clear to us in the previous § that Christian theology never understood by the innate and inborn character of the knowledge of God that man brought along any conscious knowledge of God at birth; but it thereby indicated only that the knowledge of God does not need to be imparted to man by force or violence, nor by logical reasoning or compelling proofs, but that it belongs to man naturally, spontaneously, by itself. A man, developing normally, comes without force or effort, spontaneously, to some knowledge of God. Therefore the cognitio Dei insita does not stand in opposition to the acquisita, for in a wider sense the first may also be called acquisita. For to the cognitio Dei, both insita and acquisita, the revelation of God precedes. He does not let himself go unwitnessed. He works with his eternal power and divinity from all sides upon man, not only from without but also from within; in nature and human world, in heart and conscience, in prosperity and adversity God, as it were, meets man. And this one, created after God’s image, received the capacity to receive the impressions thereof and thereby to come to some awareness and some knowledge of the Eternal Being. The cognitio insita, as soon as it is cognitio and thus not only potentia but actus, has never arisen otherwise than under the operation of the revelation of God in and outside man and is thus always insofar acquisita. For these reasons the distinction between the cognitio Dei insita and acquisita was formerly usually indicated otherwise. It consisted in these two things. First, in this: that the cognitio Dei innata was obtained spontaneously, of itself, without effort or force, the cognitio Dei acquisita, on the other hand, by reasoning and argument, by reflection and proof along the viae causalitatis, eminentiae et negationis; the former was noetica, the latter dianoetica. And secondly, from this flowed forth also this difference: that the first consisted only in principles and was universal and necessary, but the second was more elaborated and developed, delivered more concrete propositions and was therefore also subjected to all kinds of doubt and dispute. That there is a God stood and stands firm for nearly all. But the proofs for the existence of God have been found by thinking and are therefore alternately despised and highly esteemed, Moor, Comm. I 41. 44. And therewith the distinction is rightly indicated. It does not lie in this, that the innate knowledge of God would arise from man and the acquired from the world. Also the moral proof is derived from the moral consciousness that is found within man. In both it is the same and the whole revelation of God, from which the cognitio Dei penetrates into our consciousness. But in the cognitio Dei insita that revelation works upon the consciousness of man and breeds there impressions and perceptions. In the cognitio Dei acquisita that revelation of God is thought through by man; his understanding goes to work, his reflection awakens, and with clear, distinct consciousness he seeks by reasoning and proof to climb up from the creatures to God. For man has in no area of knowledge enough of impressions and perceptions. He is not satisfied with consciousness alone. It is not sufficient for him, _that_ he knows. He wants also _to know_, that he knows; he endeavors to explain the _how_ and _why_ of his knowing. And hence, that the ordinary, everyday, empirical knowing always seeks to pass over into the proper, scientific knowing; that faith elevates itself to theology, and the cognitio Dei insita completes itself in the cognitio Dei acquisita.


2. The division into cognitio Dei insita and acquisita is usually applied only to the theologia naturalis. And this is then distinguished from and often opposed to the theologia revelata. Already earlier this view has been extensively contested, I 219–244. There is no separate theologia naturalis that could be obtained by man outside all revelation from the thinking contemplation of the world. That knowledge of God which is summarized in the theologia naturalis is no product of human reason. It presupposes first of all that God himself reveals himself in the works of his hands; it is not man who seeks God, but God who seeks man also through his works in nature. Furthermore, it is not man who with the natural light of reason understands and knows this revelation of God; all religions of the heathen are positive; but on man’s side there is need of a sanctified understanding and an opened eye to observe God, the true and living God, in his creatures. And even this is not enough. Also the believer, also the Christian would not understand God’s revelation in nature and could not represent it purely, if God himself had not described in his Word how and what he reveals of himself in all the world. The cognitio Dei naturalis is taken up in Scripture itself and extensively unfolded therein. Therefore it is also a wholly wrong method that the Christian, as it were, rids himself of the special revelation in Scripture and of the illumination of the Holy Spirit in treating the theologia naturalis, discusses it without any Christian Voraussetzung, and then proceeds to the theologia revelata. The Christian stands also as a dogmatician from the beginning with both feet upon the foundation of special revelation; not first in the locus de Christo but also in the locus de Deo he is a Christian believer. But standing thus, he looks around him, and armed with the spectacles of Holy Scripture he sees in all the world a revelation of that same God whom he knows and confesses in Christ as his Father in heaven. Then, however, by cognitio Dei insita and acquisita is not to be understood that knowledge which we obtain outside special revelation from creation. From our earliest youth on, God’s special revelation in Christ has worked upon us all; we are born in the covenant of grace and thus as Christians; all kinds of Christian influences have formed the knowledge of God which now is our portion, in much greater measure than the operation that has proceeded from God’s revelation in nature upon our heart; and in the light of that special revelation we have all also learned to contemplate nature and the world around us. We all owe the knowledge of God from nature to God’s special revelation in his Word. Nature would let us hear just as confused voices as the heathen, if we had not listened to God’s speech in the works of grace and thereby now understood his voice also in the works of nature. But now that revelation of God in nature is also of high value. It is the same God who speaks to us in nature and in grace, in creation and re-creation, in the Logos and in Christ, in the Spirit of God and in the Spirit of Christ. Nature and grace are no opposition; we have one God, from whom and through whom and to whom both are.


3. This applies to both the cognitio Dei insita and acquisita; both we owe to Holy Scripture. It is true that it takes no trouble to prove the existence of God. It proceeds from that existence and presupposes that man knows and acknowledges God. It considers man not yet so deeply sunk that he needs a proof beforehand in order to believe. For he is still God’s image-bearer, God’s offspring, and endowed with a νους to observe in the creation God’s eternal power and divinity. The denial of God’s existence is in the eye of Scripture a sign of folly, of deep moral decline, Ps. 14:2. Such people are the exception, not the rule. Generally Scripture counts on such who acknowledge the existence of God unforced and by virtue of the prompting of their nature. It appeals not to the reasoning understanding, but to the rational and moral consciousness. It analyzes and argues not, but lets God be seen in all the works of his hands. But that it does then indeed. Heaven and earth together with all creatures, foliage and grass, rain and drought, fruitful and unfruitful years, food and drink, health and sickness, riches and poverty, and all things speak to the pious of God. There is no part, no piece of the world, in which his power and divinity is not beheld. To heaven and earth, birds and ants, flowers and lilies man is referred, to behold and acknowledge God. Scripture does not reason in the abstract and does not make God the conclusion of a syllogism, leaving it now to us whether we shall find that proof compelling. But it speaks with authority, it proceeds theologically and religiously from God, it lets his virtues be seen in his works and it demands that we shall acknowledge God. An ox knows its owner, and a donkey the manger of its master, but Israel has no knowledge, my people understands not, Isa. 1:3. It stands firm for it that God reveals himself in the creatures and that he lets himself go unwitnessed to no one, Acts 14:17, Rom. 1:19. And thus appealing to the whole creation, as a testimony and revelation of God, it contains everything in germ which later was broadly elaborated in the proofs and dialectically unfolded. There is truth in the observation of C. J. Nitzsch, Syst. der Chr. Lehre, 6te Aufl. S. 142, that Holy Scripture gives us a beginning and analogy of the aitiological proof in Rom. 1:20, of the teleological in Ps. 8; 19; Acts 14:17, of the moral in Rom. 2:14 and of the ontological in Acts 17:24, Rom. 1:19, 32. But speaking thus, not in dialectical argument but in the language of testimony, not appealing to the reasoning understanding but making an appeal to heart and conscience, to all the rational and moral consciousness of man, Scripture has never been without power and influence. Also in this it is a powerful word, sharp as a two-edged sharp sword, piercing to the dividing of soul and spirit, a judge of the thoughts and intents of the heart. Also without logical argument and philosophical proof Scripture is mighty in this its testimony, because it is the word of God and finds support in the rational and moral nature of every man. It is God himself who lets himself go unwitnessed to no one. And it is man himself who, because he was created after God’s image, must give hearing and assent to this testimony in spite of himself. In this light must also the so-called proofs for God’s existence be viewed. That will preserve us both from overestimation and from contempt of the proofs.
