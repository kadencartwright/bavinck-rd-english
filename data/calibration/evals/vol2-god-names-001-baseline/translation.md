§ 23. THE NAMES OF GOD.


1. All that of God becomes knowable to us in His revelation is designated in Holy Scripture by the Name of God. The original meaning of the word שֵׁם is probably that of sign, mark, σημα, signum, just as the Greek ὀνομα and the Latin nomen are derived from the stem gno and thus indicate that by which something is known, a distinguishing mark. A name is a sign of him who bears it, an appellation according to one or another property in which he reveals himself and becomes known. There is a connection between the name and its bearer, and that connection is not arbitrary but grounded in the bearer himself. Even with us, and still today, when names have mostly become sounds without sense, that connection is still felt. A name is something personal and entirely different from a number or a specimen of its kind. It is always more or less unpleasant when our name is written or used incorrectly. Upon the name hangs our honor, our worth, our person and individuality. But much stronger was that connection in earlier times, when names still had a transparent meaning and were a revelation of the person or the thing that received it. Thus it is also in Scripture. Adam had to give names to the animals according to their nature, Gen. 2:19, 20. Of many names in Scripture the meaning is indicated and at the same time the reason why they are given, e.g., Eve, Gen. 3:20, Cain, 4:1, Seth, 4:25, Noah, 5:29, Babel, 11:9, Ishmael, 16:11, Esau and Jacob, 25:25, Moses, Ex. 2:10, Jesus, Mt. 1:21, etc. Repeatedly a name is changed or a surname added when a person appears in another quality, Abraham, Gen. 17:5, Sarah, 17:15, Israel, 32:28, Joshua, Num. 13:16, Jedidiah, 2 Sam. 12:25, Mara, Ruth 1:20, Peter, Mk. 3:16, etc. After His ascension Christ has received a name above every name, Phil. 2:9, Heb. 1:4, and to the believers in the new Jerusalem a new name is given, Op. 2:17, 3:12, 22:4. The same applies to the name of God. There is an intimate connection between God and His name. This connection too is according to Scripture not accidental or arbitrary but laid by God Himself. Men do not name Him: He gives Himself a name. Therefore the name stands in the foreground as revelation from God's side, in active and objective sense, as nomen editum. Then the name of God is identical with the virtues or perfections which He reveals outwardly, with His glory, Ps. 8:2, 72:19, His honor, Lev. 18:21, Ps. 86:11, 102:16, His redeeming power, Ex. 15:3, Jes. 47:4, His service, Jes. 56:6, Jer. 23:27, His holiness, 1 Chr. 18:10, Ps. 105:3, 3:21, etc. The name is God Himself, as He reveals Himself in one or another relation. That name as revelation of God is therefore great, Ezech. 36:23, holy, Ezech. 36:20, terrible, Ps. 111:9, a high tower, Ps. 20:2, a strong tower, Spr. 18:10. With proper names, specifically with the name Ihvh, God has made Himself known to Israel. Through the Malak, in whose innermost being His name is, Ex. 23:20, He has revealed Himself to Israel. And through Him He has laid His name upon the children of Israel, Num. 6:27, established the remembrance of His name among them, Ex. 20:24, set His name among them and caused it to dwell, Deut. 11:14, 12:5, particularly in the temple, which is built for His name, 2 S. 7:13. In that temple now dwells His name, 2 Chr. 20:9, 33:4. Through that name He redeems, Ps. 54:3, and for that name's sake He cannot forsake Israel, 1 S. 12:22, Jes. 48:9, 11, Ps. 31:4, 23:3, 143:11 f. But Israel may not therefore blaspheme, profane, or use in vain that name, Ex. 20:7, Lev. 18:21, 24:11. On the contrary, that name must be called upon, declared, magnified, known, confessed, feared, exalted, waited for, sought, hallowed, Gen. 4:26, 12:2, Ex. 9:16, Deut. 28:58, 1 K. 8:33, Ps. 5:12, 34:4, 52:11, 83:17, 122:4, Jes. 26:8, Mt. 6:9, Joh. 12:28, etc. In the N.T. there is but one name through whom God grants salvation; it is the name of Jesus Christ, Hd. 4:12. But in that one name He has also fully revealed Himself; therein is comprehended all that God is and shall be for His own; in that name He communicates Himself, with all His benefits, to His people, Mt. 28:19, Luk. 24:47, Joh. 20:31, Hd. 2:38, 1 Cor. 6:11, Jak. 5:14. And an even richer revelation do the believers have to expect in the new Jerusalem, Op. 3:12, when His name shall be upon everyone's forehead, Op. 22:4. The name of God is in Holy Scripture the designation, not of God as He exists in Himself, but of God in His revelation, in His various relations to the creatures. This name is, however, not arbitrary, but God reveals Himself thus and in this way because He is who He is. Upon His name therefore hangs His honor, His renown, all His virtues, His entire revelation, His own divine Being. But that name therefore grants to him to whom it is revealed special privileges and at the same time lays upon him peculiar obligations. The name of God includes that He, revealing Himself therein, is now also named thereby by creatures. The nomen editum passes over into the nomen inditum. The εἰναι and καλεισθαι are in Holy Scripture two sides of the same thing. God is who He is called, and He is called who He is. What He reveals of Himself is expressed and represented in particular names. He grants to His creatures the privilege of naming and addressing Him on the ground of and in conformity with His revelation. The one name of God, as the comprehensive term of His entire revelation both in nature and grace, falls apart for us into many, many names. Only in this way do we obtain a survey of the richness of His revelation and the deep meaning of His name. We name Him and may name Him according to all that of His Being is revealed in creation and re-creation. But all those names, as appellations of God, lay upon us the obligation to sanctify and glorify them. It is the one name, the full revelation, and insofar the own Being of God Himself, with which we have to do in all those names. God places Himself through His name in a particular relation to us; thereto we ought to respond in our relation to Him, Schultz, Altt. Theol.⁴ 513 f. Oehler, Theol. des A. T. § 56. art. Name in Herzog². Smend, Lehrb. S. 281 f. Cremer, s. v. ὀνομα.


2. The names by which God is named and addressed by us are therefore not arbitrary; they are not devised by us according to our own pleasure. It is God Himself who consciously and freely reveals Himself in nature and grace, who grants us the right to name Him on the ground of this revelation, yes who in His Word has made His own names known to us on the ground of His revelation. All these names now bear without distinction this character, that they are derived from revelation. There is not a single name that indicates God's Being an sich. The nomen editum is the foundation of all nomina indita. And since the revelation of God in nature and Scripture is specifically directed to men, therefore it is a human language in which God speaks of Himself to us; therefore they are human words of which He makes use; therefore they are human forms under which He appears. In Scripture, therefore, there do not occur here and there some anthropomorphisms; the whole Scripture is anthropomorphistic. From the first to the last page it testifies of a coming of God to and a seeking of man. The entire revelation of God concentrates itself in the Logos, who has become σαρξ, and is as it were one incarnation, one becoming-human of God. If God spoke to us in divine manner, no creature would understand Him, but this is His grace, which already began with creation, that He bows Himself down to His creatures and speaks to them and appears to them in human manner. Therefore all the names by which God names Himself and lets Himself be named by us are derived from earthly and human relations. Thus He is called in Scripture El, the Strong One, Elshaddai the Almighty, Jahveh, the Being One, further Father, Son, Spirit, good, merciful, gracious, righteous, holy, etc., all concepts which first apply to creatures and then are transferred to God in eminent sense. Even the so-called incommunicable properties, such as immutability, independence, simplicity, eternity, omnipresence are represented in Scripture in forms and expressions derived from the finite and therefore also in negative sense; eternity cannot be represented except as a negation of time. Scripture itself makes no attempt whatsoever to describe these perfections of God positively, according to their own being, thought apart from their relation to the finite. But the anthropomorphism goes much further in Scripture. All that belongs to men and even to creatures is also ascribed to God, particularly partes hominis, membra corporis, sensus, affectus, actiones, subjecta and adjuncta humana. God has a soul, Lev. 26:11, Mt. 12:28, and a Spirit, Gen. 1:2, etc. Of a body of God there is never mention, although God in Christ also assumed a true body, Joh. 1:14, Col. 2:17, and the church is called Christ's body, Ef. 1:22. But all bodily organs are nevertheless ascribed to God. There is mention of His face, Ex. 33:20, 23, Jes. 63:9, Ps. 16:11, Mt. 18:10, Op. 22:4, of His eyes, Ps. 11:4, Hebr. 4:13, of His eyelids, Ps. 11:4, of His eyeball, Deut. 32:10, Ps. 17:8, Zach. 2:8, of His ears, Ps. 55:2, of His nose, Deut. 33:10, of His mouth, Deut. 8:3, of His lips, Job 11:5, of His tongue, Jes. 30:27, of His neck, Jer. 18:17, of His arm, Ex. 15:16, of His hand, Num. 11:23, of His right hand, Ex. 15:12, of His finger, Ex. 8:19, of His heart, Gen. 6:6, of His bowels, Jes. 63:15, Jer. 31:20, Luk. 1:78, of His bosom, Ps. 74:4, of His lap, Joh. 1:28, of His foot, Jes. 66:1. Furthermore there is no human emotion or it is also present in God, such as joy, Jes. 62:5, rejoicing, Jes. 65:19, grief, Ps. 78:40, Jes. 63:10, displeasure, Ps. 95:10, Jer. 7:18, 19, fear, Deut. 32:27, love with all its modifications such as mercy, compassion, grace, longsuffering, etc., further zeal and jealousy, Deut. 32:21, repentance, Gen. 6:6, hatred, Deut. 16:22, wrath, Ps. 2:5, vengeance, Deut. 32:35. Further all human actions are also transferred to God, such as knowing, Gen. 18:21, investigating, Ps. 7:10, knowing, Gen. 3:5, thinking, Gen. 50:20, forgetting, 1 Sam. 1:11, remembering, Gen. 8:1, Ex. 2:24, speaking, Gen. 2:16, calling, Rom. 4:17, commanding, Jes. 5:6, rebuking (chastising, reproving), Ps. 18:16, 104:7, answering, Ps. 3:5, testifying, Mal. 2:14; resting, Gen. 2:2, working, Joh. 5:17; seeing, Gen. 1:10, hearing, Ex. 2:24, smelling, Gen. 8:21, tasting, Ps. 11:4, 5; sitting, Ps. 9:8, arising, Ps. 68:2, going, Ex. 34:9, coming, Ex. 25:22, walking, Lev. 26:12, descending, Gen. 11:5, meeting, Ex. 3:18, visiting, Gen. 21:1, passing by, Ex. 12:13, forsaking, Richt. 6:13; writing, Ex. 34:1, sealing, Joh. 6:27, engraving, Jes. 49:16; striking, Jes. 11:4, chastising, Deut. 8:5, punishing, Job 5:17, binding up, Ps. 147:3, healing, Ps. 103:3, healing, Deut. 32:39, killing and making alive, Deut. 32:39; wiping away, Jes. 25:8, blotting out, 2 Kon. 21:13, washing, Ps. 51:4, cleansing, Ps. 51:4, anointing, Ps. 2:6, adorning, Ezech. 16:11, clothing, Ps. 132:16, crowning, Ps. 8:6, girding, Ps. 18:33; destroying, Gen. 6:7, devastating, Lev. 26:31, killing, Gen. 38:7, plaguing, Gen. 12:17, judging, Ps. 58:12, condemning, Job 10:2, etc. Furthermore God is also very often indicated with names that indicate a certain calling, office, relation, relationship among men. He is a bridegroom, Jes. 61:10, a husband, Jes. 54:5, a father, Deut. 32:6, a judge, king, lawgiver, Jes. 33:22, a man of war, Ex. 15:3, a hero, Ps. 78:65, Zef. 3:17, an artist and architect, Hebr. 11:10, a farmer, Joh. 15:1, a shepherd, Ps. 23:1, a physician, Ex. 15:26, etc.; while then in these qualities there is again mention of His seat, throne, footstool, rod, scepter, weapons, bow, arrow, sword, shield, chariot, banner, book, seal, treasure, inheritance, etc. Yes even, in order to express what God is for His own, all kinds of images are borrowed from the animate and inanimate creation. He is compared to a lion, Jes. 31:4, an eagle, Deut. 32:11, a lamb, Jes. 53:7, a hen, Mt. 23:37, to the sun, Ps. 84:12, the morning star, Op. 22:16, to a light, Ps. 27:1, a lamp, Op. 21:23, a fire, Hebr. 12:29, a spring or fountain, Ps. 36:10, a cistern, Jer. 2:13, to food, bread, drink, water, ointment, Jes. 55:1, Joh. 4:10, 6:35, 55, to a rock, Deut. 32:4, a hiding place, Ps. 119:114, tower, Spr. 18:10, a high tower, Ps. 9:10, a shadow, Ps. 91:1, 121:5, a shield, Ps. 84:12, a way, Joh. 14:6, a temple, Op. 21:22, etc., Polanus, Synt. Theol. II c. 35, Alsted, Theol. schol. 104 sq. Glassius, Philol. Sacra 1691 p. 1116-1181.


3. The whole creation, all nature with all its realms, the world of men above all, are in Holy Scripture made serviceable to the description of the knowledge of God. No limit is set to the anthropomorphism. All creatures, living and lifeless, animate and inanimate, organic and inorganic, offer names to bring the greatness of God somehow to our consciousness. Although ἀνωνυμος in Himself, God is nevertheless πολυωνυμος in His revelation. Omnia possunt dici de Deo, et nihil digne dicitur de Deo. Nihil latius hac inopia. Quaeris congruum nomen, non invenis; quaeris quoquo modo dicere, omnia invenis, August., tract. 13 in Ev. Joh. n. 5. And in clarification of why God can be named in so many ways, Augustine made use of a striking image. Our body has various needs, for light and air, food and drink, dwelling and clothing, etc., and all these things are different and lie in the creatures next to one another. Our spirit also has many and various needs, but what provides for it is not various but is always the one and same Divine Being. In terra aliud est fons, aliud lumen. Sitiens quaeris fontem, et ut pervenias ad fontem, quaeris lucem, et si dies non est, accendis lucernam, ut ad fontem pervenias. Fons ille ipse est lux: sitienti fons est, caeco lux est; aperiantur oculi ut videant lucem, aperiantur fauces cordis ut bibant fontem; quod bibis hoc vides, hoc audis. Totum sit tibi Deus, quia horum quae diligis totum tibi est. Si visibilia attendis, nec panis est Deus, nec aqua est Deus, nec lux ista est Deus, nec vestis est Deus, nec domus est Deus. Omnia enim haec visibilia sunt et singula sunt; quod est panis non hoc est aqua, et quod est vestis, non hoc est domus, et quod sunt ista, non hoc est Deus, visibilia enim sunt. Deus tibi totum est; si esuris, panis tibi est; si sitis, aqua tibi est; si in tenebris es, lumen tibi est, quia incorruptibilis manet; si nudus es, immortalitatis vestis tibi est, cum corruptibile hoc induerit incorruptionem et mortale hoc induerit immortalitatem, tract. 13 in Ev. Joh. Likewise Pseudo-Dionysius says that God is ἀνωνυμος and at the same time πολυωνυμος, He is at the same time παντα τα ὀντα και οὐδεν των ὀντων, de div. nom. c. I. § 6. 7. In Thomas we read, quod Deus in se praehabet omnes perfectiones creaturarum, quasi simpliciter et universaliter perfectus, S. Theol. I qu. 13 art. 2. And more beautifully still in Bonaventure: ut laudibus Deum possimus extollere, atque ad Dei cognitionem promoveamur, necessaria nobis sunt translationes ad divina. Ratio vel finis translationis duplex est. Una in qua est laus Dei, alia manuductio intellectus nostri. Propter laudem Dei necessaria est translatio. Et quoniam Deus multum est laudabilis, ne propter inopiam vocabulorum contingeret cessari a laude, sacra Scriptura docuit nomina creaturarum ad Deum transferri; et hoc in numero indefinito, ut sicut omnis creatura laudat Deum, sic Deus laudatur ex omni nomine creaturae, et qui non poterit uno nomine laudari, tamquam superexcellens omne nomen, laudaretur ex omni nomine. Alia ratio est manuductio intellectus nostri. Quia cum per creaturas ad cognoscendum creatorem venimus, ut plurimum, fere omnes creaturae habent proprietates nobiles, quae sunt ratio intelligendi Deum, ut leo fortitudinem, agnus mansuetudinem, petra soliditatem, serpens prudentiam et consimilia; ideo oportuit plura nomina transferri ad Deum, Sent. I dist. 34 art. 1 qu. 4. And Calvin agreed with this, when he said: nulla est mundi particula, in qua non scintillae saltem aliquae gloriae ipsius emicare cernantur. God is immanent in all the created. The pure in heart see God everywhere. All is full of God. Fateor pie hoc posse dici, modo a pio animo proficiscatur, naturam esse Deum, Inst. I 5, 1. 5. But there is an ascent in the creatures. The place and the rank which the creatures occupy is determined by their kinship to God. All creatures name a name of God. But among all creatures man stands at the top. He alone bears the high name of image, son, child of God. He alone is called God's offspring. And therefore the most and loftiest names of God are borrowed from man. Never, however, may man be detached from nature. And never may any creature or any part of the world be placed next to or opposite God. There is nothing outside and without God. Indeed here sin has been repeatedly committed against this. The dualism of Plato, the Neoplatonism, the Gnosticism, the Manichaeism limited God's revelation and set a matter hostilely against Him. And in various forms this exercised influence and worked after in theology. If in modern times under the influence of Kant and Jacobi the revelation of God is limited to the religious and ethical; if Scripture is recognized only in its religious-ethical content; if the seat of religion is sought only in heart or conscience, in mind or will, there is in all this one and the same dualistic principle at work. Nature, with its elements and forces, man in his social and political life, art and science come to stand outside the revelation of God and become neutral, that is, Godless territories. Naturally, then the O.T. and a very great part of the N.T. can no longer be appreciated; that nature and world have nothing more to say to the believer; that the revelation, that the Word of God loses all influence on public life; that religion, pushed back to a hidden nook of heart and chamber, forfeits all claim to respect; that dogmatics, specifically also the locus de Deo, shrinks from day to day, and that theology no longer knows how to maintain its place. Theology no longer knows how to speak of God, because it does not speak out of and through Him. It has no more names with which to name God. God becomes the great Unknown and the world becomes first ἀθεος, then ἀντιθεος.
